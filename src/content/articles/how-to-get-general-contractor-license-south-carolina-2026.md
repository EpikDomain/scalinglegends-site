## Who Needs a General Contractor License in South Carolina?

Let's make this crystal clear. ### The $5,000 Threshold Explained

That $5,000 mark is critical. It's not just your profit margin; it's the total cost of the project, including all materials, labor, subcontractors, and any other associated expenses. For most contractors, this means virtually every job you touch will require you to be licensed. Don't underestimate how quickly costs add up, even on seemingly small projects.

### General Contractor Classifications in South Carolina

The SC Contractors' Licensing Board (llr.sc.gov/clb) doesn't just issue one "General Contractor" license. They categorize licenses into different "Groups" based on the financial capacity of your business, which in turn dictates the maximum contract value you can undertake. This system ensures that contractors have the financial backing to complete the projects they're licensed for. You'll apply for a specific Group based on your financial statement.

Here's a breakdown of the General Contractor Groups and their corresponding contract limits (as of early 2026, always verify with LLR for the latest):

- **Group 1:** Maximum single contract limit of $100,000

- **Group 2:** Maximum single contract limit of $200,000

- **Group 3:** Maximum single contract limit of $400,000

- **Group 4:** Maximum single contract limit of $800,000

- **Group 5:** Unlimited single contract limit

Your financial statement, specifically your net worth and working capital, will determine which Group you qualify for. We'll dive into those specifics later, but understand that aiming for a higher Group means demonstrating greater financial stability.

Beyond the Groups, there are various classifications and sub-classifications for general contractors in South Carolina, such as:

- Building (BD)

- Highway (HW)

- Public Utilities (PU)

- Specialty (SP - with numerous sub-categories like Concrete, Framing, Masonry, etc.)

Most general contractors primarily operate under the "Building" classification, but you can apply for multiple classifications if your work scope demands it. Be precise about the classifications you need on your application; it dictates what work you're legally allowed to perform.

## Step-by-Step: Getting Your South Carolina General Contractor License

Alright, let's get down to brass tacks. This is the roadmap to getting your South Carolina General Contractor license. Follow these steps methodically, and you'll navigate the process efficiently.

### Step 1: Meet Experience and Education Requirements

The SC Contractors' Licensing Board is looking for qualified professionals. While there isn't a strict "four-year degree" requirement for general contractors, you do need to demonstrate practical experience. Typically, this means at least **two years of experience in construction directly related to the classification you're applying for** (e.g., building construction for a Building contractor) within the last five years. This experience should be verifiable and gained at a supervisory level or as a self-employed contractor.

You'll need to document this experience clearly on your application. Be ready to provide names, contact information, and descriptions of projects and responsibilities. The Board takes this seriously, so don't pad your resume; provide accurate, verifiable information.

### Step 2: Solidify Your Business Entity in South Carolina

Before you even touch the license application, you need to decide how your business will be structured. Are you operating as a sole proprietorship, a partnership, an LLC, or a corporation? For most contractors, an LLC or corporation offers liability protection that a sole proprietorship doesn't. This is a crucial business decision.

If you choose to form an LLC or corporation, you'll need to register your business with the **South Carolina Secretary of State in Columbia**. This involves filing articles of organization (for an LLC) or articles of incorporation (for a corporation). You'll also need to obtain an Employer Identification Number (EIN) from the IRS, even if you don't plan to have employees immediately. This is your business's federal tax ID, essential for banking and tax purposes.

Don't skip this. The SC Contractors' Licensing Board will require your legal business name and entity type on your application, and it needs to match what's on file with the state.

### Step 3: Prepare Your Financial Statement

This is where your chosen Group classification comes into play. You need to demonstrate financial solvency appropriate for the level of work you want to perform. You'll need to submit a financial statement prepared by a Certified Public Accountant (CPA) or a licensed public accountant. This statement must show your current assets, liabilities, net worth, and working capital.

The specific net worth and working capital requirements vary significantly by Group:

- **Group 1 ($100,000 limit):** Minimum net worth of $10,000 and working capital of $10,000.

- **Group 2 ($200,000 limit):** Minimum net worth of $20,000 and working capital of $20,000.

- **Group 3 ($400,000 limit):** Minimum net worth of $40,000 and working capital of $40,000.

- **Group 4 ($800,000 limit):** Minimum net worth of $80,000 and working capital of $80,000.

- **Group 5 (Unlimited limit):** Minimum net worth of $150,000 and working capital of $150,000.

*(Note: These figures are based on typical requirements and are subject to change by the SC Contractors' Licensing Board. Always confirm the latest requirements directly from llr.sc.gov/clb.)*

The financial statement must be current, typically within one year of your application date, and must be reviewed or audited by the CPA. A simple compilation won't cut it for higher groups. This isn't just a formality; it's how the Board ensures you have the financial stability to complete contracts without leaving clients in the lurch.

### Step 4: Submit Your Application to the SC Contractors' Licensing Board

Once you have your experience documented, your business entity established, and your financial statement prepared, it's time to complete the official application. You can find the forms and instructions on the SC Contractors' Licensing Board website (llr.sc.gov/clb). Read every instruction carefully.

The application will require:

- Your chosen business entity name and details.

- The specific classifications (e.g., Building) and Group (e.g., Group 3) you are applying for.

- Detailed documentation of your construction experience.

- The CPA-prepared financial statement.

- Personal information for all principals, officers, and members of your company.

- A non-refundable application fee.

Double-check everything before submission. Incomplete applications are the fastest way to delay your licensing process. The Board is thorough, and they will send it back if anything is missing or incorrect.

### Step 5: Conquer the South Carolina Exams

After your application is approved by the Board, you'll receive authorization to take the required examinations. In South Carolina, you generally need to pass two exams:

- **Business Management and Law Exam:** This covers state-specific business practices, contract law, licensing laws, safety regulations, and financial management pertinent to contractors in South Carolina.

- **Trade Exam:** This exam tests your technical knowledge specific to the classification you're applying for (e.g., Building, Highway, etc.).

These exams are administered by PSI Services LLC. You'll need to schedule them directly with PSI after receiving your authorization from the SC Contractors' Licensing Board. We'll dive into more detail on exam prep shortly.

### Step 6: Secure South Carolina Specific Bonds and Insurance

Once you've passed your exams, the Board will likely require you to provide proof of adequate bonding and insurance. This includes:

- **Surety Bond:** The Board may require a surety bond, especially for certain classifications or if your financial statement is borderline for your desired Group. The amount of the bond varies, but it's designed to protect consumers in case you fail to complete a contract or meet your obligations.

- **General Liability Insurance:** This is non-negotiable. You need to carry general liability insurance to protect against claims of property damage, bodily injury, and advertising injury that may arise from your contracting operations. South Carolina has minimum requirements, which we'll cover.

- **Workers' Compensation Insurance:** If you have employees in South Carolina, workers' compensation insurance is generally mandatory. This covers medical expenses and lost wages for employees injured on the job.

Obtain certificates of insurance and bond documents from your providers and submit them to the SC Contractors' Licensing Board as instructed.

### Step 7: Receive Your South Carolina License

Once you've cleared all the hurdles – application approval, successful exam completion, and submission of all required bonds and insurance – the SC Contractors' Licensing Board will issue your South Carolina General Contractor license. Congratulations! Now you're officially part of the Palmetto State's construction elite. Keep that license visible and renewed.

## Deep Dive: South Carolina Exam Requirements and Prep

The exams are often the biggest hurdle for new applicants. Don't underestimate them. They test not just your construction knowledge but also your understanding of South Carolina's specific business and legal landscape for contractors. The exams are administered by PSI Services LLC.

### The Business Management and Law Exam

This exam is standard for almost all contractor classifications in South Carolina. It's an open-book test, but that doesn't mean it's easy. You need to know the reference materials inside and out to find answers quickly. Key topics include:

- **Licensing Requirements:** SC-specific laws, classifications, renewals, and prohibited acts.

- **Business Organization:** Types of entities, registration with the SC Secretary of State.

- **Estimating and Bidding:** Cost analysis, bid preparation, contracts.

- **Contract Management:** Lien laws (SC-specific!), change orders, dispute resolution.

- **Project Management:** Scheduling, quality control, safety (OSHA standards).

- **Financial Management:** Accounting principles, [cash flow](/article/5-cash-flow-mistakes-that-kill-construction-companies), taxes (SC sales tax, income tax).

- **Labor Laws:** SC employment laws, workers' compensation, payroll.

- **Environmental and Safety:** EPA, OSHA, lead-based paint rules, asbestos.

The approved reference materials are typically listed in the candidate bulletin provided by PSI. Get these books, tab them, and familiarize yourself with their contents.

### The Trade Exam

The trade exam is specific to your chosen classification (e.g., Building, Highway, Public Utilities). Like the Business and Law exam, it's generally open-book. It tests your technical proficiency and understanding of best practices relevant to that trade.

For the **Building (BD) classification**, you can expect questions on:

- Site work, foundations, and concrete.

- Masonry, metals, and carpentry.

- Roofing, doors, and windows.

- Finishes, specialties, and equipment.

- Mechanical, electrical, and plumbing systems (general knowledge, not trade-specific licensing).

- Safety and project planning.

Again, the PSI candidate bulletin will provide a detailed content outline and a list of approved reference materials, which often include the International Building Code (IBC), OSHA standards, and other industry-specific manuals.

### Recommended Prep Resources for South Carolina Candidates

- **PSI Candidate Information Bulletin:** This is your bible. Download it from the PSI website. It contains the exam content outlines, number of questions, time limits, passing scores, and the exact list of approved reference books.

- **Approved Reference Books:** Purchase or rent all the required books. Don't cheap out here. These are the sources from which the questions are drawn.

- **Exam Prep Courses:** Many reputable schools and online providers offer courses specifically designed for the South Carolina contractor exams. These courses can help you organize the material, understand key concepts, and practice with simulated questions. Look for South Carolina-specific courses, as state laws are a significant portion of the Business and Law exam.

- **Practice Tests:** Utilize practice exams to get comfortable with the format and timing. This will help you identify areas where you need more study.

Don't just read the books; understand them. Practice navigating them efficiently to find information quickly, which is crucial for open-book exams.

## South Carolina Financial Requirements: What You Need to Show

We touched on this earlier, but it bears repeating because it's a make-or-break requirement. The SC Contractors' Licensing Board (llr.sc.gov/clb) uses your financial statement to determine your eligibility for a specific Group classification. This isn't about how much cash you have in your pocket; it's about your business's overall financial health.

### Net Worth and Working Capital Requirements by Group

As a reminder, here are the general financial benchmarks for each Group:

- **Group 1:** $10,000 Net Worth & $10,000 Working Capital

- **Group 2:** $20,000 Net Worth & $20,000 Working Capital

- **Group 3:** $40,000 Net Worth & $40,000 Working Capital

- **Group 4:** $80,000 Net Worth & $80,000 Working Capital

- **Group 5:** $150,000 Net Worth & $150,000 Working Capital

**Net Worth** is your total assets minus your total liabilities. It's the equity in your business. **Working Capital** is your current assets minus your current liabilities. It shows your ability to cover short-term obligations. Both are crucial indicators of financial stability.

### Preparing Your Financial Statement

Your financial statement must be prepared by a **Certified Public Accountant (CPA) or a licensed public accountant**. For higher groups (typically Group 3 and above), the Board usually requires a "reviewed" or "audited" financial statement, not just a "compiled" one. A review or audit provides a higher level of assurance regarding the accuracy of your financial data.

The statement must be current, generally dated within 12 months of your application submission. Ensure your CPA understands the specific requirements of the SC Contractors' Licensing Board to avoid delays. They should be familiar with preparing statements for licensing purposes.

## South Carolina Reciprocity Agreements: Expanding Your Reach

Good news for experienced contractors licensed in other states: South Carolina does have reciprocity agreements, but they are specific. The SC Contractors' Licensing Board has agreements with certain states, primarily for the **Business Management and Law exam**, not necessarily the trade exam.

As of early 2026, South Carolina typically has reciprocity agreements for the Business and Law exam with:

- **North Carolina**

- **Georgia**

- **Tennessee**

- **Mississippi**

- **Alabama**

- **Louisiana**

- **Arkansas**

- **Florida**

- **West Virginia**

**What this means:** If you hold a current, active license in good standing in one of these reciprocal states and have passed their equivalent business and law exam, you might be able to waive the South Carolina Business Management and Law exam. However, you will still need to pass the South Carolina trade exam for your specific classification.

**Important:** Reciprocity is never guaranteed. You must meet all other South Carolina licensing requirements, including financial statements, experience, and submitting a complete application to the SC Contractors' Licensing Board. Always contact the Board directly (llr.sc.gov/clb) to confirm current reciprocity agreements and eligibility before you apply.

## Business Entity Formation in South Carolina: Setting Up Shop

This is a foundational step. How you structure your business dictates everything from liability to taxation. Most serious contractors in South Carolina opt for an LLC or a corporation for the legal protections they offer.

### Registering Your Business in Columbia

If you're forming an LLC, Corporation, or Partnership (other than a sole proprietorship using your personal name), you must register your business with the **South Carolina Secretary of State in Columbia**. This involves:

- **Name Availability Search:** Ensure your desired business name isn't already taken.

- **Filing Articles of Organization (LLC) or Articles of Incorporation (Corporation):** These documents officially create your business entity in the state.

- **Appointing a Registered Agent:** This is a person or entity located in South Carolina who will receive legal and tax documents on behalf of your business.

The SC Secretary of State's website provides all the necessary forms and instructions. This process is generally straightforward but crucial for legal operation.

### EIN, SC Tax ID, and Other Essentials

- **Employer Identification Number (EIN):** As mentioned, get this from the IRS. It's your federal tax ID and is required for opening business bank accounts, hiring employees, and filing taxes.

- **South Carolina Tax ID:** You'll likely need to register with the South Carolina Department of Revenue for state tax purposes, especially if you'll be collecting sales tax on materials or have employees.

- **Business Bank Account:** Keep your personal and business finances separate. Open a dedicated business checking account as soon as your entity is formed.

- **Local Business Licenses:** Don't forget local requirements! Most cities and counties in South Carolina require their own business licenses or permits to operate within their jurisdiction. Check with the city hall or county administrative office where your primary office is located and where you plan to work.

## South Carolina Insurance Minimums: Protect Your Business

Operating a [construction business](/article/how-to-scale-a-construction-business-without-losing-control) without proper insurance in South Carolina is like building a house without a foundation – a recipe for disaster. Don't skimp here. The SC Contractors' Licensing Board and state law require specific coverages.

### General Liability Insurance

This is your primary shield. General Liability (GL) insurance protects your business from claims of bodily injury or property damage caused by your operations, products, or services. It also covers legal defense costs if you're sued.

While the SC Contractors' Licensing Board doesn't specify a universal minimum dollar amount for GL that's tied to your license application, it's generally understood that a reputable contractor will carry at least **$1,000,000 per occurrence / $2,000,000 aggregate** in general liability coverage. Many clients, especially commercial ones, will require this level of coverage in their contracts. Get a certificate of insurance from your provider to submit with your application if requested, or to keep on file for the Board.

### Workers' Compensation Insurance in South Carolina

If you have employees (even one!) in South Carolina, workers' compensation insurance is generally mandatory. The South Carolina Workers' Compensation Commission (wcc.sc.gov) oversees these requirements. This insurance covers medical expenses and a portion of lost wages for employees who suffer work-related injuries or illnesses.

**Exemptions are very limited.** Don't assume you're exempt. If you have any employees, get this coverage. Failing to carry workers' comp when required can result in severe penalties, including fines and stop-work orders. Sole proprietors and partners typically aren't required to cover themselves, but if you incorporate or form an LLC and are an employee of your own company, you might need to cover yourself.

## Common Mistakes South Carolina Applicants Make (and How to Avoid Them)

I've seen it all. Here are the most common ways contractors trip up during the South Carolina licensing process. Learn from others' errors:

- **Incomplete Applications:** This is number one. Missing signatures, unanswered questions, or absent documentation will get your application sent right back, delaying everything. *Solution:* Read the instructions carefully, double-check every item, and use the checklist provided by the SC Contractors' Licensing Board.

- **Incorrect Financial Statement:** Submitting a financial statement that doesn't meet the Group requirements or isn't prepared by a qualified CPA. *Solution:* Work with a CPA who understands the Board's specific "reviewed" or "audited" requirements and ensure your finances meet the chosen Group's minimums.

- **Lack of Verifiable Experience:** Vague descriptions or inability to verify claimed experience. *Solution:* Be specific about your roles, responsibilities, and project types. Have contact information ready for those who can verify your experience.

- **Underestimating the Exams:** Assuming open-book means easy. It doesn't. Time management and knowing how to quickly find information in the reference books are key. *Solution:* Invest in quality prep courses and practice tests. Study the reference materials thoroughly.

- **Ignoring Local Requirements:** Focusing only on the state license and forgetting city/county business licenses. *Solution:* Always check with local municipalities where you plan to operate.

- **Waiting Until the Last Minute:** The application process, review, exam scheduling, and license issuance all take time. *Solution:* Start early. Assume delays and build in buffer time.

## Maintaining Your South Carolina License: Renewals and CE

Getting your license is just the first step. Keeping it active and in good standing is equally important. In South Carolina, General Contractor licenses issued by the SC Contractors' Licensing Board must be renewed regularly.

- **Renewal Cycle:** Licenses typically need to be renewed every two years. The Board will send renewal notices, but it's ultimately your responsibility to know your expiration date.

- **Renewal Fees:** There will be a renewal fee, which can vary by classification and Group.

- **Continuing Education (CE):** As of early 2026, the SC Contractors' Licensing Board does not generally require continuing education for General Contractor license renewals. However, this can change, so always verify the current requirements on llr.sc.gov/clb before your renewal period. Even if not required, staying current with industry best practices, safety regulations, and new technologies is just good business.

Failing to renew on time can lead to late fees, an inactive license, or even requiring you to reapply from scratch. Set reminders and stay on top of it.

## FAQs About Getting a South Carolina General Contractor License

### How long does it take to get a contractor license in South Carolina?

The timeline varies significantly based on how prepared you are and how quickly the Board processes applications. Generally, expect anywhere from **2 to 4 months** from the time you submit a complete application to receiving your license. This includes application review, exam scheduling, exam passing, and final license issuance. Delays often occur due to incomplete applications or waiting for exam results.

### Can I work on small projects (under $5,000) without a license in SC?

Yes, you can. Projects with a total cost of materials and labor that do not exceed $5,000 generally do not require a state General Contractor license. However, you might still need local business licenses or permits from the city or county where the work is being performed. Always check local requirements.

### What happens if I work without a license in South Carolina?

Operating as a General Contractor for projects over $5,000 without a valid license in South Carolina is a serious offense. Penalties can include substantial fines (up to $5,000 per violation), stop-work orders, inability to enforce contracts, and even potential criminal charges. It's simply not worth the risk.

### Do I need to renew my SC General Contractor license?

Yes, South Carolina General Contractor licenses must be renewed, typically every two years. The SC Contractors' Licensing Board will send renewal notices, but it's your responsibility to ensure timely renewal to avoid late fees or license inactivation.

### What are the costs involved in getting an SC contractor license?

Costs include:

- Application fees (non-refundable, varies by classification/group).

- Exam fees (for both Business & Law and Trade exams).

- Costs for reference books and exam prep courses.

- CPA fees for preparing your financial statement.

- Business entity registration fees with the SC Secretary of State.

- Surety bond costs (if required).

- Insurance premiums (General Liability, Workers' Comp).

- Local business license fees.

These can add up, so budget accordingly. Expect several thousand dollars in total for all fees, prep, and initial insurance/bond costs, depending on your chosen Group and business structure.

### Does South Carolina offer any waivers for the exams?

South Carolina offers reciprocity for the Business Management and Law exam with several other states (e.g., NC, GA, FL) if you hold a current, active license in good standing and have passed an equivalent exam there. However, you will almost always need to pass the South Carolina Trade Exam specific to your classification. Always verify current reciprocity agreements and eligibility directly with the SC Contractors' Licensing Board.
