## The Mechanics of VA Construction Loans for Builders

To successfully capture the veteran housing market, a contractor must first understand that they are not just building a structure; they are fulfilling a federal guarantee. Unlike conventional construction loans where the buyer pays down to 20% before construction begins, VA Construction Loans allow for a one-time construction-to-permanent financing model with zero down payment. This is a massive benefit for the borrower, but for the contractor, it introduces a complex layer of lender verification. The lender acts as the escrow agent for the loan funds, meaning the contractor is not paid directly by the homeowner but rather by the lender at specific milestones.

These milestones are strictly defined. You cannot simply bill for "framing." You must submit a progress inspection request, the lender's approved inspector must verify the work, and only then is funds released. This process typically introduces a 5 to 7-day lag time between work completion and cash receipt. If your business is cash-poor, this lag is dangerous. For a company managing $5 million in annual volume, a single delayed disbursement can impact your ability to pay subcontractors or order materials for the next job.

| Mechanical Installations | 15-20% | Plumbing, Electrical, HVAC Certs |
| Certificate of Occupancy | 25-30% | Final Inspection, VA Approval |

Contractors must also account for the VA Funding Fee, which ranges from 2.15% to 3.3% for new construction. While this fee is typically paid by the veteran, it can sometimes be rolled into the loan amount, capping the total loan value. This cap affects your bid pricing strategy. If you estimate the build at $400,000 but the VA appraisal comes in at $380,000, the difference becomes a budgeting hole for the borrower or a change order for you. Navigating this discrepancy requires a contractor who is experienced in [VA eligibility and bonding](/article/va-eligibility-bonding/) processes to ensure the contract holds firm despite appraisal variances.

Furthermore, the VA requires the builder to be certified. This isn't just a paperwork formality; it is a vetting process that ensures you can deliver a safe, high-quality home. The certification validates that your company has the financial capacity to complete the build if the borrower defaults. ## [Cash Flow](/article/5-cash-flow-mistakes-that-kill-construction-companies/) and Lien Rights in VA Projects

Perhaps the most critical operational challenge in VA construction loans is the flow of funds. Unlike private construction loans where the lender may disburse funds more frequently or based on a draw schedule that favors the builder, VA loans are designed to protect the veteran. This means the payment process is often more conservative. The veteran's funds are held in escrow and released upon verification of work. For a contractor, this means a delay between incurring costs and receiving reimbursement.

Contractors must anticipate a minimum 14-day lag between submitting a draw request and receiving funds. This requires a working capital buffer of at least 20% of the total contract value to maintain payroll and material orders. If you are scaling your business, you cannot rely solely on the next job's funds to pay for the current job's materials. You must establish a revolving credit line with your bank specifically designated for VA draw gaps. This prevents the risk of lien rights being compromised due to delayed payments to suppliers.

Lien rights in VA projects are nuanced. Generally, you cannot lien a VA property in the traditional sense because the land is often owned by the government or held in trust. Instead, the lender guarantees payment. If a dispute arises, the recourse is against the lender's insurance or the surety bond, not the land itself. This makes the quality of your lien waiver releases crucial. You must obtain conditional lien waivers upon submission of a request and unconditional waivers upon receipt of payment.

Failure to manage lien waivers correctly can result in "double payment" risks or legal complications. A smart way to mitigate this is through automation. Using a system that tracks lien waivers and draw requests ensures you never release an unconditional waiver before the money hits your account. This operational discipline protects your margin and your legal standing. Furthermore, change orders on VA builds require lender approval. If you exceed the budget due to a change order, the lender may require a new appraisal or a reduction in the veteran's contribution. This creates friction in the project timeline. To avoid this, all change orders must be priced and approved before work begins. Use a standardized change order process that automatically alerts your project manager and the client if the budget variance exceeds 5%. This transparency builds trust and prevents the "sticker shock" that often kills deals in this sector.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Are VA construction loans only for buying existing homes?

No, VA construction loans can be used for new construction, rehabilitation, or building from scratch. This is a critical distinction for contractors because new builds often involve different bonding requirements and lien waiver processes compared to rehabs. Understanding this allows you to [position your company](/article/the-iija-countdown-131-billion-in-infrastructure-money-and-208-days-to-position-your-company/) as a capable builder for the 2026 market rather than a renovation specialist.

### How long does a VA draw inspection take during the build?

Typically, VA draw inspections require a turnaround time of 5 to 7 days. This timeline can fluctuate depending on the lender and the availability of the approved inspector. Contractors must budget their schedules to accommodate this gap to avoid idle crews and delayed material orders that impact the project's completion date.

### Do I need a specific license to build for VA borrowers?

While state licensing is mandatory, you also need to be listed on the VA's Approved Builder List. This requires providing proof of insurance, bonding capacity, and a clean safety record. The certification process verifies that you meet the federal standards required to protect the government-backed loan, which is different from general state contractor registration.

### Can veterans finance the purchase of land with a VA loan?

Yes, but it is more complex. A veteran can borrow to purchase land, but the loan proceeds must be used to construct a residence within 90 days. This puts pressure on the contractor to start work quickly. If you are bidding on land acquisition projects, ensure your contract includes a strict start-date clause to avoid funding expiration issues.

### What is the impact of OSHA violations on VA builder certification?

OSHA violations can directly disqualify a builder from certification or renewals. The VA reviews a company's safety history as part of the certification application. A single significant citation regarding worker safety can trigger a review that delays or denies your ability to take on these projects, highlighting the importance of safety management.

### Can change orders increase the loan amount without lender approval?

No, lenders strictly cap the loan amount based on the appraisal. Any change order that increases the cost beyond the appraised value must be covered by the borrower's out-of-pocket funds. Contractors must communicate clearly with the borrower that cost overages are not automatically covered by the loan to prevent disputes at the closing stage.
