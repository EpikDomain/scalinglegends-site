## Business Entity Formation in Tennessee

Before you lift a hammer, you need to set up your business properly. This isn't just paperwork; it's about protecting your personal assets and setting up your company for success and compliance in Tennessee.

### Registering with the Tennessee Secretary of State

Your first stop for formal business registration will be the [Tennessee Secretary of State, Business Services Division](https://sos.tn.gov/business-services), located right here in Nashville. This is where you'll register your business name and choose your legal structure.

### LLC vs. Corporation: What Makes Sense in Tennessee?

- **Limited Liability Company (LLC):** This is often the preferred choice for many new construction companies in Tennessee. An LLC offers personal liability protection, meaning your personal assets (your home, savings) are generally shielded from business debts and lawsuits. It also provides flexibility in how you're taxed. You can elect to be taxed as a sole proprietorship (if you're a single-member LLC), a partnership, or even an S-Corporation or C-Corporation for federal tax purposes.

- **Corporation (S-Corp or C-Corp):** Corporations also offer strong personal liability protection. A C-Corporation is taxed separately from its owners and can face "double taxation" (corporate profits taxed, then dividends to shareholders taxed). An S-Corporation avoids double taxation by passing profits and losses directly to the owners' personal income without being subject to corporate tax rates. Many construction companies elect S-Corp status for federal tax savings, particularly when owners can pay themselves a reasonable salary and take remaining profits as distributions.

- **Sole Proprietorship/Partnership:** While simpler to set up, these structures offer no personal liability protection. If your business is sued, your personal assets are at risk. Given the inherent risks in construction, we strongly advise against these structures once you start taking on significant projects, especially those requiring a state license.

Consult with a Tennessee business attorney and a CPA who understands the construction industry. They can guide you on the best structure for your specific situation, considering liability, growth plans, and Tennessee's tax implications.

### Tennessee Tax Implications for Business Entities

While Tennessee is famous for its lack of a state individual income tax, businesses are subject to other state taxes:

- **Franchise Tax:** This is a tax on a company’s net worth or the book value of its real and tangible personal property in Tennessee, whichever is greater. The rate is $0.25 per $100 of the tax base, with a minimum tax of $100.

- **Excise Tax:** This is a tax on a company’s net earnings or income from business done in Tennessee. The rate is 6.5% of net earnings.

These taxes apply to most corporations, LLCs, and other entities doing business in Tennessee. You'll file these with the Tennessee Department of Revenue.

## Tennessee Contractor Licensing Requirements

This is where many new contractors stumble. Tennessee's licensing system is robust, and for good reason. It protects consumers and ensures qualified professionals are building our state. Pay close attention.

### The Tennessee Board for Licensing Contractors

The authority for contractor licensing in Tennessee is the [Tennessee Board for Licensing Contractors](https://www.tn.gov/commerce/regulations/contractors/licensing/contractors-home.html). They are located in Nashville. Their rules are clear: if your project (including materials and labor) is $25,000 or more, you MUST have a state contractor's license.

### Key Requirements for a Tennessee Contractor's License

- **Application:** Submit a comprehensive application to the Board. This isn't just a form; it's a detailed submission about your company and your qualifications.

- **Experience:** You or your qualifying agent must demonstrate adequate experience in the classifications you're applying for.

**Financial Statement:** This is critical. You must submit a financial statement prepared by a Certified Public Accountant (CPA).
        

            - For projects under $1,500,000, a Reviewed Financial Statement is typically required.

            - For projects $1,500,000 and above, an Audited Financial Statement is required.

        

        Your net worth (or working capital, depending on the classification) directly determines your monetary license limit. The Board uses a formula to establish the maximum value of projects you can undertake. The higher your net worth, the higher your license limit.

    
- **Proof of Insurance:** You'll need to show proof of General Liability insurance and Workers' Compensation insurance (or an exemption, if applicable).

**Exam Requirement:**
        

            - **Business and Law Exam:** All applicants for a prime contractor's license must pass the "Business and Law" exam. This covers Tennessee-specific laws, regulations, contracts, and business practices.

            - **Trade Exam:** Depending on the classifications you're seeking (e.g., Residential, Commercial, Heavy Construction, Highway, Electrical, Plumbing), you may also need to pass one or more trade-specific exams. These test your technical knowledge.

        

        Exams are administered by PSI Services, LLC, and you'll need to schedule and pay for these separately after your application is approved to test.

    
**License Classification:** You must apply for specific classifications based on the type of work you intend to do. Common classifications include:
        

            - BC (Building Construction - Commercial)

            - Residential (for single-family and small multi-family)

            - CE (Construction Engineering - Heavy Construction, Highway, Railroad)

            - MU (Municipal Utility)

            - LLE (Limited Licensed Electrician - for electrical work under $25,000)

            - LLP (Limited Licensed Plumber - for plumbing work under $25,000)

            - Environmental, HVAC, Masonry, Roofing, etc.

        

    

Navigating the licensing process can be complex. Consider hiring a Tennessee-based licensing service or consultant who specializes in contractor applications to ensure everything is submitted correctly the first time.

## Tennessee-Specific Insurance Requirements

Insurance isn't just a good idea in construction; it's legally mandated for many aspects of operating in Tennessee, and essential for protecting your business from catastrophic losses.

### General Liability Insurance (GL)

General Liability insurance protects your business from claims of bodily injury or property damage caused by your operations, products, or services. While the state doesn't set a universal minimum coverage amount for all contractors, the Board for Licensing Contractors will require proof of GL insurance to issue a license. Most GCs, developers, and municipalities you work for will require higher limits (e.g., $1 million per occurrence, $2 million aggregate) before they'll even consider you for a project.

### Workers' Compensation Insurance (WC)

Tennessee has specific rules regarding Workers' Compensation:

- **Mandatory Coverage:** If you have five or more employees (including full-time, part-time, and seasonal employees), you are legally required to carry Workers' Compensation insurance.

- **Construction Industry Exception:** In the construction industry, this threshold drops. If you have any employees, you are required to have Workers' Compensation coverage. Some specific exceptions exist for sole proprietors and partners who elect to be excluded, but this can be risky and impact your ability to work for larger GCs.

- **Sole Proprietors/Partners/LLC Members:** If you are a sole proprietor, partner, or an LLC member, you can elect to be excluded from coverage, but be aware that if you hire even one employee, you must obtain coverage. Many GCs will still require you to carry WC coverage, even if you're legally exempt, to mitigate their own risk.

- **Subcontractor Implications:** If you hire uninsured subcontractors, you (the primary contractor) can be held liable for their employees' injuries. Always verify your subcontractors' Workers' Comp coverage.

Workers' Comp costs vary significantly based on your specific trade classifications (e.g., roofing is higher risk than interior trim work), your payroll, and your claims history. Work with an independent insurance agent in Tennessee who specializes in construction to get accurate quotes and ensure compliance.

### Bonding

Bonding is often required for larger projects, especially public works:

- **Bid Bonds:** Guarantee that if your bid is accepted, you will enter into the contract and provide the required performance and payment bonds.

- **Performance Bonds:** Guarantee that you will complete the project according to the contract terms and specifications.

- **Payment Bonds:** Guarantee that you will pay your subcontractors and suppliers.

For any state-funded project in Tennessee, and often for larger private projects, you will be required to be bonded. Your ability to get bonding is tied to your company's financial health, experience, and creditworthiness. Establish a relationship with a Tennessee surety bond agent early on.

## Tennessee Tax Obligations

Understanding your tax obligations in Tennessee is crucial for financial planning and avoiding penalties.

### No State Individual Income Tax (But Business Taxes Apply)

As mentioned, Tennessee does not have a state individual income tax. This is a significant draw for business owners and employees alike. However, this doesn't mean you're off the hook for all state taxes.

### Sales Tax on Materials

As a construction company, you will likely be considered the "consumer" of the materials you purchase for most projects. This means you generally pay Tennessee sales tax on those materials when you buy them from suppliers. The state sales tax rate is 7%, plus local sales tax, which can vary by county and city (often an additional 2.25% to 2.75%).

If you are selling tangible personal property (e.g., you operate a retail storefront selling building materials), then you would collect sales tax from your customers. However, for typical construction services, you pay the sales tax on materials you incorporate into a project.

### Franchise and Excise Tax

Reiterating this because it's a significant one for businesses: most corporations, LLCs, and other entities doing business in Tennessee are subject to the Franchise and Excise Tax. This is filed with the Tennessee Department of Revenue and is based on your net worth (Franchise Tax) and net earnings (Excise Tax).

### Federal Taxes

Don't forget your federal tax obligations, including federal income tax, self-employment tax (for sole proprietors and partners), and employer payroll taxes (Social Security, Medicare, federal unemployment tax) if you have employees. Consult with a Tennessee-based CPA to ensure you're compliant with both state and federal tax laws.

## Tennessee Prevailing Wage Requirements on Public Work

If you plan to bid on state-funded projects in Tennessee, you need to understand prevailing wage laws.

### "Little Davis-Bacon" Act

Tennessee has its own "Little Davis-Bacon" Act (T.C.A. § 12-4-401 et seq.) that applies to state-funded public works projects. This law requires contractors and subcontractors on these projects to pay their laborers and mechanics a prevailing wage rate, which includes a basic hourly rate and fringe benefits.

### Key Points for Tennessee Prevailing Wage

- **Applicability:** Applies to construction, alteration, or repair of public buildings or public works for the State of Tennessee or any political subdivision thereof.

- **Wage Determinations:** The Tennessee Department of Labor and Workforce Development (TDLWD) issues prevailing wage determinations for different crafts and localities. These rates are published and must be posted at the job site.

- **Certified Payroll:** Contractors are typically required to submit certified payroll records to the contracting agency, demonstrating compliance with the prevailing wage rates.

- **Compliance:** Failure to comply can result in penalties, back wage payments, and even debarment from future public contracts.

Always review the specific contract documents for any state or local government project to determine if prevailing wage requirements apply and to obtain the correct wage determinations for your project location and trades. Ignorance is not an excuse.

## Tennessee Workers' Compensation Requirements and Costs

We touched on this earlier, but it warrants a deeper dive because getting this wrong can have severe financial and legal repercussions in Tennessee.

### The "Five Employee" Rule (with a Construction Caveat)

The general rule in Tennessee is that employers with five or more employees are required to carry Workers' Compensation insurance. However, for employers in the construction industry, this threshold is effectively ONE employee. If you have any employees performing construction work, you are generally required to carry Workers' Compensation coverage.

### Who Counts as an Employee?

- Full-time, part-time, seasonal, and temporary employees all count.

- Independent contractors, if misclassified, can be deemed employees by the state, leading to significant penalties. Ensure your independent contractor agreements meet Tennessee's legal standards.

### Exemptions for Owners/Partners/LLC Members

Sole proprietors, partners in a partnership, and members of an LLC can elect to exclude themselves from Workers' Compensation coverage. To do this, you must file a "Notice of Exclusion" with the Tennessee Bureau of Workers' Compensation. However, remember:

- This only excludes *you*, not any employees you hire.

- Many general contractors and project owners will *still* require you to carry Workers' Comp, even if you're legally exempt, as part of their contractual requirements to protect themselves.

### Costs of Workers' Compensation

Workers' Comp premiums in Tennessee are calculated based on several factors:

- **Classification Codes (NCCI Codes):** Every type of construction work has an associated NCCI (National Council on Compensation Insurance) code, each with a specific rate. Roofing has a much higher rate than clerical office work, for example. Accurate classification is crucial.

- **Payroll:** The premium is calculated as a rate per $100 of your payroll for each classification.

- **Experience Modification Rate (EMR):** After a few years in business, your company will develop an EMR. This is a multiplier that adjusts your premium up or down based on your claims history compared to other similar businesses. A low EMR (below 1.0) means fewer claims and lower premiums; a high EMR (above 1.0) means more claims and higher premiums. Maintaining a strong safety program is key to managing your EMR.

- **Deductibles and Discounts:** You might be able to choose higher deductibles to lower premiums or qualify for safety program discounts.

Work with an experienced Tennessee insurance broker who specializes in construction. They can help you navigate the complex classification codes, understand your EMR, and find the most competitive rates while ensuring you meet all state requirements.

### Do I need a license to do small construction jobs in Tennessee?

No, not necessarily a *state* contractor's license. In Tennessee, a state contractor's license is only required for projects that are $25,000 or more (including materials and labor). ### What is the minimum insurance for a contractor in Tennessee?

While specific state-mandated minimums for General Liability insurance don't apply to all contractors, the Tennessee Board for Licensing Contractors will require proof of GL for licensing. ### How long does it take to get a contractor's license in Tennessee?

The timeline can vary. After submitting a complete application to the Tennessee Board for Licensing Contractors, it can take several weeks for processing and approval to sit for exams. ### What is the Franchise and Excise Tax in Tennessee?

The Franchise and Excise Tax is a combined tax that applies to most businesses operating in Tennessee (corporations, LLCs, partnerships, etc.). The Excise Tax is 6.5% of your net earnings from business done in Tennessee, and the Franchise Tax is $0.25 per $100 of your net worth or the book value of your tangible property in Tennessee, whichever is greater, with a minimum tax of $100. These are state-level business taxes you'll pay to the Tennessee Department of Revenue.
