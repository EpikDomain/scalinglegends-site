**General contractor license California** requirements are among the most thorough in the country, but that works in your favor. The barrier to entry keeps fly-by-night operators out of the market and creates real value for licensed contractors who put in the work to qualify. If you are serious about building a legitimate construction business in California, getting your Contractors State License Board (CSLB) license is the most important professional step you will take.

This guide walks through every requirement, every fee, the exam, the application process, and the most common mistakes that get applicants rejected or delayed. By the end, you will have a complete picture of exactly what it takes to become a licensed general contractor in California in 2026.

## Key Takeaways

- **Four years of journeyman-level experience** are required before you can apply for a CSLB general contractor license, and at least one of those years must be in a supervisory role.
- **The CSLB administers two exams**: a Law and Business exam that every applicant must pass, and a trade exam specific to your license classification.
- **Bonds and insurance are mandatory**: a $25,000 contractor license bond plus workers compensation coverage and general liability are all required before your license activates.
- **Application fees start around $450** for the initial application, with additional costs for fingerprinting, bond premiums, and insurance that can push total first-year costs well past $1,500.
- **License renewal is required every two years**, and continuing education in law and business is required starting with the second renewal cycle.

## Understanding California's Contractor Licensing Landscape

California operates one of the most active contractor licensing systems in the United States. The **Contractors State License Board (CSLB)**, a division of the California Department of Consumer Affairs, regulates all construction contracting in the state. As of 2026, the CSLB issues licenses in 44 different classifications across three broad categories: General Engineering (A license), General Building (B license), and 42 specialty classifications (C licenses).

For most people asking how to become a general contractor in California, the relevant license is the **Class B General Building Contractor license**. This is the classification that allows you to take on construction projects involving at least two unrelated building trades or crafts. A Class B license does not allow you to perform specialty work (like plumbing or electrical) as the prime contractor on standalone specialty jobs, but it does allow you to subcontract those trades as part of a broader building project.

What makes California's system distinct is the combination of rigorous experience verification, a mandatory trade exam, a law and business exam, and ongoing oversight by the CSLB after licensing. The CSLB actively investigates complaints, audits compliance, and can discipline or revoke licenses. That enforcement mechanism is exactly why a California contractor license carries weight with clients.

The other key thing to understand is that California ties licensing to individuals, not just companies. When you apply, a **Responsible Managing Officer (RMO)** or **Responsible Managing Employee (RME)** must qualify the license with their experience and pass the exams. That person's credentials are what the license is built on, so protecting that individual and their license is critical to your business continuity.

Any construction project in California with a contract value over **$500** (including labor and materials) requires the contractor to hold a valid CSLB license. Working without a license on projects above this threshold is a misdemeanor, and the CSLB runs sting operations specifically to catch unlicensed work.

## Types of General Contractor Licenses in California

Understanding the available license types before you apply will help you choose the right classification for your intended business.

**Class A: General Engineering Contractor**

This license is for contractors whose principal business involves fixed works requiring specialized engineering knowledge and skill. Examples include grading, paving, pipelines, dams, tunnels, and similar infrastructure. Class A work is heavy civil construction, not residential or commercial building.

**Class B: General Building Contractor**

This is the license most residential and commercial construction companies pursue. A Class B contractor can build, alter, repair, or improve structures where the project requires at least two unrelated building trades. If you are building houses, tenant improvement projects, commercial shells, or mixed-use construction, Class B is your classification.

**Class B-2: Residential Remodeling Contractor**

Introduced as a more accessible path for residential remodelers, the B-2 classification requires only one year of experience (rather than four) and allows work on existing residential structures. This is not a general contractor license in the traditional sense, but it can be a starting point.

**Class C: Specialty Contractors**

There are 42 specialty license classifications covering everything from electrical (C-10) to plumbing (C-36) to painting (C-33). Specialty contractors can only perform work in their specific trade. Many general contractors also hold one or more specialty licenses to self-perform certain scopes.

For the purpose of this guide, the focus is on the **Class B General Building Contractor license**, which is the standard path for someone who wants to run a full-scope general contracting business.

## Step-by-Step Requirements for a California General Contractor License

### Experience Requirements

The CSLB requires a minimum of **four years of journeyman-level experience** in the trade you are applying for, within the last ten years. At least one of those four years must be in a supervisory or foreman capacity.

Journeyman-level experience means you were doing the actual hands-on work of the trade, not working as a laborer or helper. If you were a carpenter, the CSLB expects you to have been doing carpentry work at a journeyman level for four years. If you are applying as a general building contractor, your experience should encompass framing, finishing, concrete, or other general building trades.

The experience must be verifiable. The CSLB will ask you to list your work history and may contact former employers or clients to verify it. If you are self-employed, you will need to provide documentation of projects you completed.

If you do not have four years of direct trade experience but you do have four years in a supervisory or project management role overseeing those trades, there is a pathway through experience as an **owner-operator** or through military training equivalency. The CSLB evaluates these on a case-by-case basis.

### Examination Requirements

Every CSLB applicant must pass two separate exams:

**1. Law and Business Exam**

This exam covers California contractor law, the Contractors State License Law, business and financial management, construction safety, and lien laws. It consists of approximately 115 multiple-choice questions, and you need a score of at least 72% to pass. The exam is administered by PSI Exams and can be taken at testing centers across California.

**2. Trade Exam (Class B General Building)**

The Class B trade exam tests your knowledge of general building construction, reading plans, specifications, materials, methods, building codes, and construction safety. It contains approximately 120 questions and also requires a 72% score to pass. You have three and a half hours to complete each exam.

If you have previously held a license in a related classification, you may qualify for an exam waiver for one or both exams. Contact the CSLB directly to confirm eligibility.

Exam results are valid for one year, so you need to complete your application and get approved within 12 months of passing.

### Bond Requirements

Before your license can be issued, you must file a **contractor's license bond** with the CSLB in the amount of **$25,000**. This bond protects consumers if a contractor fails to complete a project, fails to pay subcontractors or suppliers, or violates licensing laws.

The bond is not a cash deposit. You purchase it from a licensed surety company, and the annual premium is typically **1% to 3% of the bond amount**, meaning most contractors pay between $250 and $750 per year depending on their credit history.

If your license is classified as qualifying a business entity (corporation or LLC), additional qualifying individual bonds may be required. Consult the CSLB's bond requirements page for entity-specific requirements.

### Insurance Requirements

California requires licensed contractors to carry **workers compensation insurance** if they have any employees, even part-time. There is no state-mandated minimum for general liability insurance, but most clients, general contractors, and property owners will require proof of general liability coverage before awarding contracts.

Industry standard general liability limits for a Class B contractor are **$1 million per occurrence and $2 million aggregate**, though larger projects and commercial clients may require higher limits.

If you have no employees and are a sole proprietor, you may be eligible to file a Certificate of Self-Insurance for workers compensation, but consult an insurance professional before assuming you qualify.

## Exam Preparation Tips

The CSLB exams are not trivial. Many applicants underestimate the depth of the Law and Business exam in particular. Here is what actually works:

**Study the California Contractor License Law book.** The CSLB publishes this reference material, and a significant portion of the Law and Business exam draws directly from it. Read it cover to cover, then read it again.

**Use CSLB-approved exam prep courses.** Several California-based schools offer prep courses specifically aligned with CSLB exams, including International Code Council study materials and online platforms tailored to the trade exams. Look for courses that include practice exams with rationales.

**Focus on the business side.** Many trade professionals underestimate the business and finance questions. The exam covers lien waivers, contract law, payroll taxes, insurance requirements, and OSHA safety regulations in detail. Do not skip this content.

**Take multiple practice tests under timed conditions.** The time pressure is real. Training yourself to answer 115 to 120 questions in 3.5 hours without rushing at the end takes practice.

**Book your exam early.** PSI testing centers book up, especially in Southern California. Schedule your exam date before you have finished studying so you have a fixed deadline to work toward.

If you fail an exam, you can retake it after a waiting period. The CSLB allows multiple retakes, but each attempt requires a new scheduling fee.

## Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

**Submitting incomplete experience documentation.** The CSLB returns applications that lack sufficient work history detail. Be thorough and specific when listing your experience, including employer names, addresses, dates, and your specific role on each job.

**Misunderstanding the supervisory requirement.** Four years of experience is not enough if none of it was supervisory. Confirm that at least one of your qualifying years demonstrates that you were directing the work of others, not just doing the work yourself.

**Not studying the Law and Business content.** Contractors who are skilled tradespeople often pass the trade exam and fail the Law and Business exam. Budget significant study time for the legal and business content.

**Letting the exam score expire.** If you pass your exams but take more than 12 months to complete your application, your scores expire and you have to retake the exams. Move quickly after passing.

**Underinsuring the business.** Buying the cheapest general liability policy you can find is a false economy. A single job site accident can generate claims that exceed minimal policy limits, leaving you personally exposed.

**Not disclosing past legal or financial issues.** The CSLB conducts a background check. Failing to disclose a prior conviction or bankruptcy on your application can result in denial or revocation. Disclose and explain rather than omit.

**Qualifying under someone else's license without a written agreement.** If you are using an RMO who works for your company, have a clear written agreement about their role, compensation, and what happens if they leave. Losing your qualifier can suspend your license.

## How to Start a General Contracting Business in California

Getting licensed is step one. Building a business that can actually scale in California's competitive market requires systems, financial management, and a clear growth strategy.

Before you take on your first project as a licensed contractor, set up your business entity properly. Most California general contractors operate as an LLC or corporation rather than a sole proprietorship, primarily for liability protection. Register with the California Secretary of State, obtain an EIN, open a dedicated business bank account, and set up bookkeeping from day one.

The **U.S. Small Business Administration (SBA)** offers free resources for new contractors, including business plan templates, financial projections guidance, and connections to local SCORE mentors who have run construction businesses. Visit sba.gov for access to these tools. SBA-backed loans can also be a viable source of startup capital for equipment purchases and working capital.

Understand California's prevailing wage requirements from the start. If you intend to bid on public works projects, you will need to pay workers the prevailing wage rate for each trade and file certified payroll reports. This adds administrative complexity but opens access to a large and stable source of government contracts.

Set up your contracts, change order processes, and lien rights procedures before you need them. California has strict mechanics lien laws that protect contractors and subcontractors, but the deadlines are unforgiving. Missing a preliminary notice deadline or a lien filing window can cost you the ability to collect on a valid claim.

## HowTo: Get Your General Contractor License in California

### Step 1: Confirm Your Experience Qualifies

Verify that you have at least four years of journeyman-level experience in general building trades within the past ten years, with at least one year in a supervisory capacity. If you are short, identify what additional experience you need and how to document what you already have.

### Step 2: Choose Your License Classification

Decide whether you are applying for a Class A (General Engineering), Class B (General Building), or a specialty Class C license. For most residential and commercial contractors, Class B is the right choice.

### Step 3: Determine Your Qualifying Individual

Decide whether you are qualifying as the Responsible Managing Officer (RMO) if you own the business entity, or hiring a Responsible Managing Employee (RME). This person must meet all experience requirements and pass all exams.

### Step 4: Obtain Your Fingerprints

Complete the Live Scan fingerprinting process at an approved Live Scan provider. The results are submitted electronically to the CSLB as part of the background check. Keep your receipt as proof of submission.

### Step 5: Submit the Application

Complete the CSLB application online at cslb.ca.gov or by mail. Include all required documentation about your business entity, your qualifying individual's work history, and payment of the application fee.

### Step 6: Schedule and Pass the Exams

Once the CSLB approves your application for examination, schedule both the Law and Business exam and the Class B Trade exam through PSI. Study seriously for both, complete practice exams, and pass within the 12-month window before scores expire.

### Step 7: Obtain Your Contractor's License Bond

Purchase a $25,000 contractor's license bond from a licensed California surety company. You will receive a bond certificate that must be filed with the CSLB before your license is issued.

### Step 8: Secure Insurance Coverage

Obtain general liability insurance at appropriate coverage limits and, if you have employees, workers compensation insurance. Your insurer will provide certificates of insurance that you will submit to clients and maintain on file.

### Step 9: File the Bond and Insurance with CSLB

Submit proof of your contractor's license bond and workers compensation insurance (or a self-insurance certificate) to the CSLB. Your license will not be issued until these are on file.

### Step 10: Receive and Activate Your License

Once the CSLB processes your bond and insurance documentation, your license will be issued. You will receive a pocket card and your license number will appear in the CSLB online license database. Verify it appears correctly and begin operating.

## FAQ

### How long does it take to get a general contractor license in California?

The full process from initial application to receiving your license typically takes **three to six months**, though it can take longer depending on CSLB processing times and how quickly you schedule and pass your exams. Submitting a complete and accurate application from the start reduces delays significantly.

### Can I apply for a California contractor license if I worked in another state?

Yes. Out-of-state experience counts toward the four-year experience requirement, provided it is journeyman-level work in the same trade. You will still need to pass both CSLB exams unless you qualify for a waiver through a reciprocity arrangement. California has limited reciprocity with other states, so verify your situation directly with the CSLB.

### What happens if I work without a California contractor license?

Contracting without a valid CSLB license on projects over $500 is a misdemeanor in California. Penalties include fines of up to $5,000, and repeat violations can result in criminal prosecution. Additionally, unlicensed contractors have no right to file a mechanics lien for unpaid work, which means they have no legal recourse if a client refuses to pay.

### Do I need a separate license for each type of construction work?

Not necessarily. A Class B General Building Contractor license covers a broad range of building trades. However, you cannot perform certain specialty work (electrical, plumbing, HVAC, fire sprinklers) as a prime contractor on standalone specialty jobs without holding the corresponding specialty license. Many general contractors subcontract these trades to licensed specialists.

### What is the difference between an RMO and an RME?

A **Responsible Managing Officer (RMO)** is an officer (president, vice president, secretary, treasurer, or manager) of the licensed business entity who qualifies the license with their experience and exam scores. A **Responsible Managing Employee (RME)** is an employee of the company who qualifies the license but does not have an ownership stake. If an RME leaves the company, the license is suspended until a new qualifier is designated.

### How much does a California contractor license bond cost?

The bond amount required is $25,000. The annual premium you pay to a surety company is typically 1% to 3% of the bond amount, so between $250 and $750 per year for most applicants. Applicants with poor credit may pay higher premiums. The bond premium is an ongoing annual cost for as long as you hold the license.

### Can I get a contractor license in California with a felony conviction?

A prior felony conviction does not automatically disqualify you from a CSLB license, but it will trigger a more thorough review. The CSLB evaluates the nature of the offense, how much time has passed, evidence of rehabilitation, and the relevance of the conviction to contractor activities. Honesty on the application is critical. Failing to disclose a conviction is treated more seriously than the conviction itself in most cases.

### How do I renew my California contractor license?

CSLB licenses expire every two years on the last day of the month in the birth month of the qualifier. The renewal fee is approximately $450 (verify current fees at cslb.ca.gov). Starting with the second renewal, licensees must complete continuing education requirements in California Contractor License Law, safety, and business management. The CSLB sends renewal notices in advance, but it is your responsibility to track your expiration date.
