## Understanding Georgia's Mechanics Lien Law (O.C.G.A. §44-14-360 et seq.)

In Georgia, a mechanics lien, officially known as a "materialmen's lien" or "laborer's lien," is a powerful legal tool that allows those who furnish labor, materials, or services for the improvement of real property to secure payment. If you've contributed to a construction project and haven't been paid, a lien can attach to the improved property itself, providing a claim against its value. This gives you leverage, as the property owner or other interested parties will often be compelled to resolve the debt to clear the title.

The legal framework for these liens is primarily found in the Official Code of Georgia Annotated (O.C.G.A.) §44-14-360 through §44-14-369. This statute outlines who can file a lien, what steps must be taken, and the critical deadlines involved. Unlike some states, Georgia's lien laws are strictly construed, meaning any deviation from the statutory requirements can invalidate your claim. Precision and adherence to the letter of the law are paramount.

## Who Has Lien Rights in Georgia?

Georgia law extends lien rights to a broad range of parties who contribute to the permanent improvement of real property. These include:

- **General Contractors:** Those with a direct contract with the property owner.

- **Subcontractors:** Those who contract with [a general contractor](/article/how-to-get-general-contractor-license-florida-2026/) to perform a specific part of the work.

- **Material Suppliers (Materialmen):** Companies or individuals who provide materials, fixtures, or machinery used in the improvement of the property.

- **Laborers:** Individuals who perform manual labor on the project.

- **Architects, Engineers, and Surveyors:** Design professionals who furnish plans, drawings, or surveys for the improvement of real estate.

- **Renters of Machinery:** Those who rent equipment used on the property.

Crucially, to have lien rights, your labor, services, or materials must be used in the permanent improvement of the real property. This means temporary site offices or tools not incorporated into the final structure typically do not give rise to lien rights. Furthermore, a direct contractual relationship with the owner, general contractor, or a first-tier subcontractor is generally required, or proper preliminary notice must be given as detailed below.

## The Critical First Step: Georgia's Preliminary Notice Requirements

Georgia's preliminary notice requirements are among the most critical and often misunderstood aspects of its lien law. Failure to send the correct notice at the correct time can extinguish your lien rights entirely. The type of notice required, and when it's due, depends on whether the project is residential or commercial, and whether a Notice of Commencement has been filed.

### Notice to Contractor for Residential Projects

For those furnishing labor, services, or materials on **residential property** (defined as a single-family dwelling, duplex, triplex, or quadruplex, and any appurtenances thereto), a "Notice to Contractor" is almost always required. This notice, governed by O.C.G.A. §44-14-361.5, serves to inform the owner and general contractor of your involvement in the project. Its purpose is to protect owners from double payment and to ensure transparency regarding who is working on their property.

- **Who Must Send It:** Subcontractors, material suppliers, and anyone not in direct contractual privity with the owner.

- **Deadline:** This notice must be sent by registered or certified mail or statutory overnight delivery within **30 days** of the date you first furnish labor, services, or materials to the project.

- **Recipients:** The owner or the contractor (or both). It's always best practice to send it to both if their identities are known.

**Required Content:** The Notice to Contractor must contain:

- The name, address, and telephone number of the person or entity furnishing labor, services, or materials.

- The name and address of the general contractor.

- The name of the owner of the real estate.

- A description of the real estate (sufficient to identify it).

- A general description of the labor, services, or materials furnished or to be furnished.

**Crucial Note:** If you fail to send this Notice to Contractor within the 30-day window on a residential project, your lien rights are extinguished, regardless of whether you've been paid.

### Notice of Commencement and its Impact on Commercial Projects

For **commercial projects**, a preliminary "Notice to Contractor" is generally not required unless the owner or general contractor has filed a "Notice of Commencement" with the Clerk of Superior Court in the county where the property is located. A Notice of Commencement (also governed by O.C.G.A. §44-14-361.5) provides public notice that a project is beginning and identifies key parties.

- **If a Notice of Commencement IS Filed:** If you are a subcontractor or supplier and a Notice of Commencement has been filed for the commercial project, you must send a copy of your "Notice to Contractor" (with similar content as described for residential projects) to the general contractor and the owner identified in the Notice of Commencement within **30 days** of your first furnishing of labor, services, or materials. Failure to do so will extinguish your lien rights.

- **If No Notice of Commencement IS Filed:** If a Notice of Commencement has not been filed, then a preliminary "Notice to Contractor" is generally not required for commercial projects. However, it is always a best practice to send a voluntary preliminary notice to the owner and general contractor to establish your presence on the project and maintain clear communication. While not statutorily required in this specific scenario, it can prevent payment disputes and strengthen your position if a lien becomes necessary.

Always check the public records for a Notice of Commencement at the start of any commercial project. This simple step can save your lien rights.

## How to File a Construction Lien in Georgia: A Step-by-Step Guide

Filing a construction lien in Georgia is a precise, multi-step process. Each step must be executed flawlessly to ensure your lien is valid and enforceable. Below is a detailed breakdown:

### Step 1: Confirm Your Eligibility and Gather Documentation

Before you even begin drafting, ensure you meet the criteria for filing a lien in Georgia. You must have provided lienable labor, services, or materials to a specific property and have an outstanding balance due. Gather all relevant documentation:

- Contracts, subcontracts, and purchase orders.

- Invoices and payment applications.

- Delivery tickets and time sheets.

- Correspondence related to non-payment.

- Proof of sending any required preliminary notices (e.g., certified mail receipts).

- Accurate legal description of the property.

- Name of the property owner (as listed in public records).

- Date of last furnishing of labor or materials.

### Step 2: Draft the Claim of Lien Affidavit

The "Claim of Lien" (often referred to as an Affidavit of Claim of Lien) is the document you will file. Its content is strictly dictated by O.C.G.A. §44-14-361.1(a)(2). Missing any of these elements can invalidate your lien. The affidavit must be sworn to and signed by the lien claimant or their authorized agent.

**Mandatory Content for a Georgia Claim of Lien:**

- **Name of Lien Claimant:** Your full legal name or company name.

- **Name of Owner:** The name of the true owner of the real estate against which the lien is claimed, if known. It's crucial to identify the correct legal owner, often found through county tax assessor records.

- **Description of Real Property:** A full and accurate legal description of the property. A street address alone is usually insufficient. This description must be sufficient to identify the property on the public records.

- **Amount Claimed:** The exact amount for which the lien is claimed. This should be the unpaid balance for lienable work performed.

- **Date Last Furnished:** The date upon which the last labor, services, or materials were furnished. This is critical for calculating the 90-day filing deadline.

- **Statement of Due Claim:** A statement that the claim has become due.

- **Statement of Substantial Compliance:** A statement that the lien claimant has substantially complied with the contract, plans, and specifications, as the case may be, for the work performed or materials furnished.

- **Statement Regarding Preliminary Notice:** A statement that the lien claimant has provided the Notice to Contractor required by O.C.G.A. §44-14-361.5, if such notice was required. If no such notice was required, the lien claim should state that no Notice to Contractor was required.

- **Sworn Statement:** The claim must be sworn to by the claimant or their agent. This typically involves notarization.

Use clear, concise language. Avoid jargon where possible, but ensure legal terms are used accurately. It is highly recommended to use a template specifically designed for Georgia liens or consult with a Georgia construction attorney for drafting.

### Step 3: File the Lien with the Clerk of Superior Court

Once drafted and properly sworn, the Claim of Lien must be filed with the Clerk of Superior Court in the county where the property is located. This is the official recording office for real estate documents.

- **Filing Deadline:** The Claim of Lien must be filed within **90 days** of the date upon which the last labor, services, or materials were furnished to the property. This 90-day clock is strict. Missing it by even one day will invalidate your lien.

- **Location:** Clerk of Superior Court in the county where the improved property is situated. For example, if the property is in Fulton County, you file with the Fulton County Clerk of Superior Court.

- **Fees:** Be prepared to pay filing fees, which vary by county. Check the specific county clerk's website for current fee schedules.

Upon filing, the Clerk will record the lien and assign it a book and page number or instrument number. This officially places the lien on the property's title.

### Step 4: Serve the Claim of Lien

Filing the lien is only half the battle. You must also formally notify the property owner (and possibly the contractor) that the lien has been filed. This is known as "serving" the lien.

- **Service Deadline:** Within **2 business days** after the date the Claim of Lien is filed, you must send a copy of the filed lien to the owner of the property or the contractor, or both.

- **Method of Service:** Service must be made by registered or certified mail or statutory overnight delivery. This method provides proof of delivery, which is essential for legal compliance.

- **Recipients:** The law states "the owner of the property or the contractor, or both." To be safe, it is always best practice to serve both the property owner and the general contractor, if known, at their last known address.

Retain the proof of mailing (e.g., certified mail receipt, return receipt requested) as evidence that you fulfilled this requirement. Failure to properly serve the lien can render it unenforceable.

## Navigating Georgia's Critical Lien Deadlines

Georgia's lien law is a labyrinth of deadlines. Missing any of them, even by a single day, can be fatal to your lien rights. Here's a consolidated look at the most critical timelines:

### Preliminary Notice (Notice to Contractor) Deadline

- **For Residential Projects:** Within **30 days** of first furnishing labor, services, or materials.

- **For Commercial Projects (if Notice of Commencement filed):** Within **30 days** of first furnishing labor, services, or materials.

**Consequence of Missing:** Loss of all lien rights for the project.

### Claim of Lien Filing Deadline

- File the Claim of Lien with the Clerk of Superior Court within **90 days** of the date upon which the lien claimant last furnished labor, services, or materials to the property.

**Consequence of Missing:** Loss of all lien rights for the project.

### Lien Service Deadline

- Serve a copy of the filed Claim of Lien on the owner or contractor (or both) within **2 business days** after the date of filing.

**Consequence of Missing:** The lien may be rendered unenforceable.

### Enforcement Action (Lawsuit) Deadline

- An action to recover the amount of the claim (i.e., a lawsuit to foreclose on the lien) must be commenced within **12 months** from the date the Claim of Lien was filed. This lawsuit must be filed in the superior court of the county where the real property is located, and a "Lis Pendens" (notice of pending action) must also be filed to provide public notice of the lawsuit.

**Consequence of Missing:** The lien will expire and become unenforceable.

### The Impact of a Notice of Contest of Lien

Property owners have a mechanism to shorten the 12-month enforcement period. Under O.C.G.A. §44-14-368, an owner or contractor may file a "Notice of Contest of Lien" with the Clerk of Superior Court and serve it on the lien claimant. This notice states that the lien claimant must commence an action to enforce the lien within **60 days** from the date of receiving the Notice of Contest. If the claimant fails to file a lawsuit within this shortened 60-day period, the lien becomes unenforceable.

**Consequence of Missing:** The lien will expire and become unenforceable after 60 days from receipt of the Notice of Contest.

## Required Content of the Georgia Lien Claim

As reiterated in Step 2 of the filing process, the content of your Claim of Lien affidavit is not merely suggestive; it is legally mandated by O.C.G.A. §44-14-361.1(a)(2). To be absolutely clear, your sworn affidavit MUST include:

- The name of the lien claimant.

- The name of the owner of the real estate.

- A description of the real estate sufficient for identification. This typically means a legal description (e.g., lot and block number, metes and bounds) from the deed or property records, not just a street address.

- The amount for which the lien is claimed.

- The date upon which the last labor, services, or materials were furnished.

- A statement that the claim has become due.

- A statement that the lien claimant has substantially complied with the contract, plans, and specifications, as the case may be, for the work performed or materials furnished.

- A statement that the lien claimant has provided the Notice to Contractor required by O.C.G.A. §44-14-361.5, if such notice was required. If no such notice was required, the lien claim shall state that no Notice to Contractor was required.

- The sworn signature of the claimant or their authorized agent, typically notarized.

Any omission or inaccuracy in these elements can be grounds for a court to invalidate your lien. Be meticulous.

## Serving Your Georgia Lien: Who, What, and How

Proper service of your filed Claim of Lien is as important as the filing itself. Georgia law is explicit about the requirements:

- **Who to Serve:** The property owner is the primary recipient. It is also highly advisable to serve the general contractor if you are a subcontractor or supplier. If the owner is a corporation or LLC, serve its registered agent or principal office.

- **What to Serve:** A true and accurate copy of the *filed* Claim of Lien, bearing the Clerk's stamp, book, and page number (or instrument number).

- **How to Serve:** Service must be made via registered mail, certified mail, or statutory overnight delivery. Personal service is generally not an acceptable substitute unless explicitly agreed upon or allowed by court order. The key is verifiable proof of delivery.

- **When to Serve:** Within **2 business days** after the date the Claim of Lien is filed with the Clerk of Superior Court.

Keep all receipts, tracking numbers, and delivery confirmations. These documents are your evidence that you met the service requirements, should the validity of your lien ever be challenged.

## Enforcing Your Georgia Lien: The Lawsuit Timeline

A filed lien is a cloud on the property's title, but it's not payment. To actually recover the funds, you must enforce the lien through a lawsuit. This is known as an "action to foreclose on a mechanics lien."

- **Standard Deadline:** You must file this enforcement lawsuit within **12 months** from the date your Claim of Lien was filed. This lawsuit is typically filed in the Superior Court of the county where the property is located.

- **Lis Pendens:** Concurrently with filing the lawsuit, you must also file a "Lis Pendens" (notice of pending action) with the Clerk of Superior Court. This document serves as public notice that a lawsuit affecting the title to the property is ongoing.

- **Notice of Contest:** As discussed, if the owner or contractor files a "Notice of Contest of Lien" and properly serves it on you, your deadline to file the enforcement lawsuit shortens dramatically to **60 days** from the date you receive that notice. This is a common tactic to force a quick resolution or invalidate the lien.

If you miss the 12-month (or 60-day, if applicable) deadline, your lien will automatically expire and be invalid, and you will lose the ability to foreclose on the property. This is why active monitoring of your lien status and deadlines is crucial.

## Georgia Lien Waivers: Conditional vs. Unconditional

Lien waivers are documents exchanged for payment that waive your right to file a lien for the work covered by that payment. Georgia law, specifically O.C.G.A. §44-14-366, has specific requirements for these waivers to be enforceable. It's critical to understand the distinction between conditional and unconditional waivers:

- **Conditional Lien Waiver:** This type of waiver becomes effective *only upon actual receipt of payment*. It typically states that the waiver is conditioned upon payment of a specific amount and is effective once that payment clears. This is generally preferred for progress payments, as it protects your lien rights until you are certain the funds have cleared.

- **Unconditional Lien Waiver:** This waiver is effective *immediately upon signing*, regardless of whether you have actually received payment. Signing an unconditional waiver without receiving funds means you are giving up your lien rights for that payment period without recourse.

**Key Georgia Provisions for Lien Waivers:**

- **No Advance Waiver:** Georgia law prohibits clauses in contracts that waive lien rights in advance of furnishing labor or materials. Any such provision is unenforceable.

- **Specific Language:** For a lien waiver to be valid in Georgia, it must substantially comply with the statutory forms provided in O.C.G.A. §44-14-366. There are separate forms for interim (progress payment) waivers and final waivers, both conditional and unconditional. Always use the statutory language.

- **Signature Required:** Waivers must be signed by the claimant or their authorized agent.

Always review lien waivers carefully before signing. Never sign an unconditional waiver unless you have received and confirmed the payment. If in doubt, consult with a Georgia construction attorney.

## Bond Claims on Georgia Public Projects (The Little Miller Act)

Mechanics liens cannot be filed against public property (e.g., schools, government buildings, roads). Instead, Georgia has its own version of the federal Miller Act, known as the "Little Miller Act," codified in O.C.G.A. §36-82-101 et seq. This statute requires general contractors on most public works projects to furnish a payment bond to protect subcontractors and suppliers.

If you're working on a Georgia public project and haven't been paid, your recourse is typically a claim against this payment bond, not a lien against the property.

- **Who Can Make a Claim:** Generally, those who have a direct contract with the prime contractor, or those who have a contract with a subcontractor.

- **Notice Requirement:** If you do not have a direct contractual relationship with the prime contractor (e.g., you are a sub-subcontractor or a supplier to a subcontractor), you must give written notice to the prime contractor within **90 days** after the last day of the month in which you furnished labor or materials for which payment is claimed. This notice must state the amount claimed and the name of the party for whom the labor was performed or to whom the materials were furnished.

- **Lawsuit Deadline:** An action on the payment bond must be brought no later than **one year** after the completion of the work on the public project and the acceptance of the work by the public authority.

Like mechanics liens, bond claims have strict deadlines and notice requirements. Failure to comply will result in the loss of your right to recover under the bond.

## Common Mistakes That Invalidate Georgia Liens

Given the strict construction of Georgia's lien laws, even minor errors can be fatal. Here are some of the most common mistakes:

- **Missing a Deadline:** This is the most frequent and most severe error. Whether it's the 30-day preliminary notice, 90-day filing deadline, 2-business day service deadline, or the 12-month enforcement deadline (or 60-day if contested), missing any of them will invalidate your lien.

- **Incorrect Property Description:** Using a street address instead of a full legal description, or providing an inaccurate legal description, can render a lien unenforceable because it doesn't properly identify the property.

- **Failure to Serve Properly:** Sending the lien by regular mail, or not sending it within the 2-business day window, is a critical mistake. Proof of delivery is non-negotiable.

- **Incorrect Owner Information:** Naming the wrong property owner (e.g., the developer instead of the actual landowner) can invalidate the lien. Always verify ownership through public records.

- **Insufficient Content in the Affidavit:** Omitting any of the mandatory elements required by O.C.G.A. §44-14-361.1(a)(2) will likely result in the lien being deemed invalid.

- **Liening for Non-Lienable Items:** Including items like lost profits, attorney fees (unless specifically allowed by contract and statute), or work not permanently incorporated into the property can jeopardize the entire lien.

- **Signing an Unconditional Waiver Prematurely:** Giving up your lien rights before receiving assured payment can leave you without recourse.

- **Failing to File Lis Pendens:** If you file a lawsuit to enforce the lien, failing to file a Lis Pendens concurrently will mean the lien won't be valid against subsequent purchasers or lenders without actual notice.

## Releasing and Discharging a Georgia Lien

Once your payment dispute is resolved and you've received full payment, you have a legal obligation to release or discharge the lien. This removes the cloud on the property's title and is an important step in maintaining good business relationships.

- **When to Release:** After you have received full payment for the amount claimed in the lien.

- **How to Release:** You must file a "Cancellation of Lien" or "Release of Lien" document with the same Clerk of Superior Court where the original lien was filed. This document formally acknowledges that the lien has been satisfied and is no longer valid.

- **Required Content:** The release document should reference the original lien by its book and page number (or instrument number) and clearly state that the lien is satisfied and released.

Promptly releasing a lien after payment is a legal requirement in Georgia (O.C.G.A. §44-14-362). Failure to do so can result in penalties or even a lawsuit against you for damages caused by the unreleased lien.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ) About Georgia Construction Liens

### Can I file a lien without a written contract in Georgia?

A1: Yes, in many cases, you can still file a lien without a formal written contract, provided you can prove that you furnished labor, services, or materials to the property with the owner's knowledge and consent, and that you have an implied contract or a clear understanding of the work to be performed and the agreed-upon price. However, having a written contract significantly strengthens your position and simplifies proving your claim. It is always advisable to have a written contract.

### What happens if the property owner files for bankruptcy after I file a lien?

A2: When a property owner files for bankruptcy, an "automatic stay" typically goes into effect, which halts most collection actions, including the enforcement of a lien. However, if your lien was properly filed and perfected *before* the bankruptcy filing, it generally remains a secured claim against the property in the bankruptcy proceedings. You will likely need to file a "proof of claim" in the bankruptcy court and potentially seek relief from the automatic stay to enforce your lien. This is a complex area of law and requires immediate consultation with a Georgia bankruptcy and construction attorney.

### How is "completion of work" defined for the 90-day lien filing deadline?

A3: Georgia law defines "completion of work" as the last date upon which labor, services, or materials were furnished to the property. This is a factual determination. It generally refers to the last day you performed actual, physical work on the project or delivered materials that were incorporated into the project. Punch list items, warranty work, or minor corrective actions typically do not restart the 90-day clock. It's crucial to track your last day on site meticulously.

### Can I include attorney fees and collection costs in my Georgia lien amount?

A4: Generally, no. Georgia's mechanics lien statute is strictly construed, and it typically only allows you to lien for the value of the labor, services, or materials furnished to the property. Attorney fees, interest, and collection costs are usually not lienable items unless specifically provided for in a written contract between the parties that explicitly allows for such recovery in a lien action, and even then, their enforceability within the lien itself can be challenged. You may be able to recover these costs in a subsequent breach of contract lawsuit, but they are typically not part of the lien amount itself.

### What's the difference between a Georgia mechanics lien and a bond claim?

A5: A Georgia mechanics lien (O.C.G.A. §44-14-360 et seq.) is a claim against the *real property* itself, providing a security interest in the property's value. It is used on private [construction projects](/article/surviving-the-messy-middle-of-construction-growth/). A bond claim (under Georgia's Little Miller Act, O.C.G.A. §36-82-101 et seq.) is a claim against a *payment bond* provided by the general contractor on public construction projects. Because public property cannot be liened, the bond acts as a substitute security, guaranteeing payment to subcontractors and suppliers. The procedures, deadlines, and enforcement mechanisms for each are distinct.

### What if I supply materials to another supplier, not directly to the project or a subcontractor?

A6: In Georgia, lien rights typically extend down to the second tier (e.g., a supplier to a subcontractor). However, generally, a "supplier to a supplier" does not have lien rights. The materials must be furnished either to the owner, general contractor, or a subcontractor who is actually performing work on the project. If you supply materials to another supplier who then furnishes them to the project, you likely do not have lien rights under Georgia law.

### Can a subcontractor lien a residential property if the GC already paid them, but the owner hasn't paid the GC?

A7: This scenario touches on Georgia's "full price" lien rule versus the "defense of payment." Georgia is generally considered a "full price" lien state, meaning a lien claimant's recovery is based on the value of their work/materials, not limited by what the owner owes the general contractor. However, for residential projects, the effectiveness of the "Notice to Contractor" (preliminary notice) is crucial. If a subcontractor or supplier fails to send the required Notice to Contractor on a residential project, the owner *can* assert a "defense of payment" if they have already paid the general contractor in full. But if the Notice to Contractor was properly sent, the owner may not be able to assert this defense, and the subcontractor's lien could still be valid even if the owner paid the GC. This is a complex area where legal advice is paramount.
