## The Geotechnical Hurdle: Building on Liquefiable Soil

The San Diego International Airport South Terminal sits on a foundation that poses one of the most significant geotechnical risks in the United States: liquefiable reclaimed land. In 2026, as **construction business growth 2026** continues to demand expansion into denser urban environments, understanding these subsurface challenges is critical for any contractor bidding on similar infrastructure projects. The site consists of 15 feet of loose fill soil overlaying liquefiable soils, a scenario that turns solid ground into fluid mud during seismic events if not properly mitigated. To address this, the project team drove 3,000 steel piles to depths of 90 feet, anchoring the structure deep into stable strata. This was not a standard excavation; it required precision that rivals deep-sea drilling, necessitating rigorous compliance with OSHA excavation standards and EPA environmental regulations regarding runoff and soil displacement.

The scale of the stabilization effort was immense. Beyond the piles, the team installed more than 30 retaining walls using jet grouting to stabilize the soil mass. Jet grouting is a technique where a high-pressure jet of grout mixes with the in-situ soil to create a hardened mass, effectively locking the foundation in place. This innovation was essential because the airport must remain operational 24/7. The project team had to work around live flight operations, which dictated the schedule and safety protocols. One active runway constraint meant that all heavy equipment and material deliveries were scheduled during narrow windows where air traffic flow was managed by the FAA. ## Structural Innovation: Eliminating 100 Columns

One of the most striking achievements of the San Diego Airport project is the engineering feat that eliminated 100 structural columns within the terminal core. This was made possible through the application of Buckling Restrained Braces (BRBs), a seismic device that allows steel braces to yield in tension and compression without buckling. In traditional design, these braces are housed within steel columns, which are then encased in concrete, creating a significant visual obstruction. By using BRBs, the design team allowed the structure to absorb and dissipate seismic energy while leaving the terminal interior open and column-free. This innovation is not just an engineering marvel; it is a financial one, resulting in $58 million in saved construction costs.

The savings came from multiple sources: reduced steel tonnage (40% less steel used), reduced concrete volume, and significant labor savings associated with the fabrication and installation of the smaller structural members. The embodied carbon of the structure dropped by 30%, a massive metric in the era of LEED and the International Energy Conservation Code (IECC) where sustainability is increasingly tied to tax incentives and regulatory compliance. This structural efficiency aligns with the broader theme of **construction business growth 2026**.
