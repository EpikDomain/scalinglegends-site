## Laying the Foundation: Business Entity & Registration in Virginia

Before you even think about swinging a hammer, you need to establish your legal business identity. In Virginia, this primarily means dealing with the State Corporation Commission (SCC) in Richmond.

### Choosing Your Business Structure (LLC, S-Corp, C-Corp)

This isn't a decision to take lightly. It impacts your liability, taxation, and administrative burden. Consult with a Virginia-based attorney and accountant. Here’s a quick rundown:

- **Sole Proprietorship/Partnership:** Easiest to set up, but offers no personal liability protection. Your personal assets are at risk. Not recommended for construction due to inherent risks.

- **Limited Liability Company (LLC):** The most common choice for small to medium construction businesses. It provides personal liability protection (separating your personal assets from business debts) and offers flexible taxation (can be taxed as a sole prop, partnership, S-Corp, or C-Corp).

- **Corporation (S-Corp, C-Corp):** Offers strong liability protection. A C-Corp is subject to "double taxation" (company profits taxed, then dividends to owners taxed). An S-Corp avoids double taxation by passing profits/losses directly to owners' personal income, but has stricter operational requirements.

For most startups, an LLC taxed as a pass-through entity (or S-Corp) is the sweet spot in Virginia.

### Registering with the Virginia State Corporation Commission (SCC)

All formal business entities (LLCs, corporations) must register with the [Virginia State Corporation Commission (SCC)](https://scc.virginia.gov/). This is where you file your Articles of Organization (for LLCs) or Articles of Incorporation (for Corporations). You’ll need to choose a unique business name that hasn’t already been taken in Virginia. The SCC website allows you to search for existing names.

**Key Requirement: Registered Agent.** Every business entity in Virginia must have a registered agent physically located in the state. This agent receives official legal and tax documents on your behalf. You can be your own registered agent if you meet the criteria, but many businesses opt for a professional registered agent service to ensure compliance and privacy.

### Obtaining Your Federal EIN

Regardless of your business structure (unless you're a true sole proprietor with no employees), you'll need an Employer Identification Number (EIN) from the IRS. This is essentially your business's Social Security Number. It's free and easy to obtain directly from the [IRS website](https://www.irs.gov/).

### Local Business Licenses (BPOL Tax)

After state registration, you must register your business with the locality (city or county) where you plan to operate. Virginia doesn't have a statewide general business license. Instead, most localities impose a Business, Professional, and Occupational License (BPOL) tax. This is an annual tax based on your gross receipts, and you’ll need to apply for a license with the Commissioner of the Revenue or Treasurer’s office in your specific city or county. Don't skip this; local penalties can be steep.

## Navigating the DPOR: Virginia Contractor Licensing Requirements

This is where the rubber meets the road. Virginia takes contractor licensing seriously, and for good reason. The Department of Professional and Occupational Regulation (DPOR), through the [Virginia Board for Contractors](https://www.dpor.virginia.gov/Boards/Contractors/), manages this process. Getting your license is non-negotiable.

### Understanding Virginia's Three-Tiered Licensing System

Virginia utilizes a three-tiered system based on the monetary value of the contracts you undertake:

- **Class C License:** For contracts up to $10,000 per job, and less than $150,000 in total contracts over a 12-month period. This is the entry-level license, often suitable for very small specialized trades or handyman services.

- **Class B License:** For contracts between $10,000 and $120,000 per job, and less than $7,500,000 in total contracts over a 12-month period. This is where many growing small to medium-sized contractors start.

- **Class A License:** For contracts of any value (unlimited). This is for general contractors undertaking large commercial, residential, or public works projects.

The class of license determines the scope and scale of projects you can legally bid on and perform. Always aim for the highest class your qualifications allow, as it opens more doors.

### Eligibility Requirements

Regardless of the class, all applicants must meet certain criteria:

- **Designated Employee:** This individual is responsible for the overall management and administration of the business. They must be a W-2 employee (or an owner/officer) and pass a mandatory 8-hour pre-license education course if applying for a Class A or B license. They also handle the business law exam.

- **Qualified Individual (QI):** This is the person who meets the experience requirements for the specific specialty you're applying for (e.g., building, plumbing, electrical). They must have documented experience (2 years for Class C, 3 years for Class B, 5 years for Class A) and pass the relevant trade exam(s). The QI can be the same person as the Designated Employee, but they don't have to be.

- **Financial Stability:** All classes require demonstrating financial solvency. This can be done through a satisfactory credit report or, for Class A and B, a minimum net worth (e.g., $45,000 for Class A, $10,000 for Class B). A poor credit history might require a bond.

### The Application Process: Step-by-Step

- **Determine Your License Class & Specialties:** Based on your intended scope of work and experience.

- **Complete Pre-License Education (Class A & B):** The Designated Employee must complete the 8-hour business course.

- **Identify Your Designated Employee & Qualified Individual(s):** Ensure they meet the experience criteria.

- **Gather Financial Documentation:** Prepare credit reports or financial statements.

- **Submit the Application to DPOR:** The application is comprehensive and requires detailed information about your business, its principals, and the experience of your Qualified Individual(s). Ensure everything is accurate and complete to avoid delays.

**Pass the PSI Exams:** Once your application is approved, DPOR will authorize you to take the required exams administered by PSI.

- **Business Law Exam:** Taken by the Designated Employee for all license classes.

- **Trade Exam(s):** Taken by the Qualified Individual for the specific classification(s) (e.g., Building, Electrical, HVAC, Plumbing).

- **Receive Your License:** Once all steps are completed and passed, DPOR will issue your Virginia Contractor License.

Don't underestimate the exams. They require dedicated study. PSI offers candidate information bulletins with content outlines and practice tests. Virginia has numerous approved providers for the mandatory 8-hour business course.

## Insuring Your Enterprise: Virginia-Specific Requirements

Insurance isn't just a good idea; it's a legal and contractual necessity in Virginia's [construction industry](/article/building-roads-and-breaking-barriers-ebony-jennings/). Without proper coverage, you're exposing your business and personal assets to catastrophic risk. And no reputable GC or client will work with you.

### General Liability Insurance (GL)

While not strictly mandated by the state for all contractors, General Liability (GL) insurance is universally required by clients, lenders, and property owners. It protects you from claims of bodily injury or property damage caused by your operations, products, or completed operations. Typical minimums range from $1 million per occurrence, but for larger commercial projects or public work, $2 million or higher aggregate limits are common.

### Virginia Workers' Compensation Requirements

This is a big one. The [Virginia Workers' Compensation Commission (VWCC)](https://www.workcomp.virginia.gov/) mandates coverage for most employers.
**The Rule:** If you regularly employ two or more full-time or part-time employees, including corporate officers and LLC members, you are required to carry workers' compensation insurance. This applies even if those employees are family members.

**Exemptions:** Sole proprietors and partners who have no employees (and do not elect to be covered themselves) are generally exempt. However, if you hire even one employee, you fall under the requirement if you reach the "two or more" threshold. Many sole proprietors and partners choose to obtain coverage voluntarily for their own protection.

**Costs:** Workers' comp rates in Virginia vary dramatically based on your specific trade (NCCI classification codes), payroll, and claims history (Experience Modification Rate or EMR). Expect higher rates for high-risk trades like roofing or heavy demolition. Shop around, but prioritize reputable carriers with strong claims service.

### Bonding: Your Key to Public & Larger Private Work

Surety bonds are not insurance, but a guarantee. They are essential if you plan to bid on public works projects in Virginia or larger private contracts.

- **Bid Bonds:** Guarantee that you will enter into a contract if your bid is accepted.

- **Performance Bonds:** Guarantee that you will complete the project according to the contract terms.

- **Payment Bonds:** Guarantee that you will pay your subcontractors and suppliers.

The [Virginia Department of General Services (DGS)](https://dgs.virginia.gov/procurement/procurement-policies-procedures/), and most local government agencies, require performance and payment bonds for public projects exceeding certain thresholds (e.g., $100,000 for state projects). Building a strong relationship with a reputable surety bond agent is crucial for growth.

### Other Essential Coverages

- **Commercial Auto Insurance:** Legally required for all business vehicles.

- **Inland Marine Insurance:** Covers your tools, equipment, and materials while in transit or at a job site.

- **Commercial Property Insurance:** If you own an office or shop, this covers the building and its contents.

- **Umbrella/Excess Liability:** Provides additional liability coverage beyond your primary policies. Highly recommended for commercial contractors.

## Virginia Tax Obligations: Keeping It Compliant

Taxes are a cost of doing business. Understanding your Virginia-specific tax obligations from the start will prevent headaches down the line. You'll primarily interact with the [Virginia Department of Taxation](https://www.tax.virginia.gov/).

### State Income Tax (Corporate & Individual)

- **Corporate Income Tax:** If your business is structured as a C-Corporation, Virginia imposes a flat 6% corporate income tax on your taxable income.

- **Individual Income Tax (Pass-Through Entities):** If you operate as a sole proprietorship, partnership, LLC (taxed as a pass-through), or S-Corporation, business profits "pass through" to your personal income. You'll pay Virginia's individual income tax, which is a graduated rate currently ranging from 2% to 5.75% (as of early 2026, subject to legislative changes).

### Virginia Sales and Use Tax

This is critical for [construction companies](/article/construction-market-intelligence-march-6-2026-conexpo-unleashes-autonomous-equipment-as-agc-launches-2m-infrastructure-campaign/). The statewide retail sales and use tax rate in Virginia is 4.3%. However, most localities impose an additional local sales tax, bringing the combined rate to anywhere from 5.3% to 7% in many areas (e.g., Northern Virginia).
**The Rule:** You, as the contractor, are generally considered the "user" or "consumer" of materials and supplies incorporated into real property. This means you pay sales tax to your suppliers when you purchase materials like lumber, concrete, wiring, etc. You do NOT charge sales tax to your customer for the completed [construction project](/article/surviving-the-messy-middle-of-construction-growth/), as it's considered an improvement to real property, not a sale of tangible goods.

Keep meticulous records of all material purchases and sales tax paid. If you purchase materials out-of-state without paying Virginia sales tax, you're responsible for remitting Virginia "use tax" to the Department of Taxation.

### Employer Withholding Tax

If you have employees, you'll need to register with the Virginia Department of Taxation to withhold state income tax from their wages. You'll then remit these withheld taxes to the state on a regular basis (e.g., monthly or quarterly), along with federal withholding taxes to the IRS.

### Annual Registration Fees (No "Franchise Tax")

Virginia doesn't have a traditional "franchise tax" based on capital, but all corporations and LLCs are required to pay an annual registration fee to the State Corporation Commission (SCC). This fee varies based on your entity type and authorized shares/capital, typically ranging from $50 to $100 for most small businesses.

## Prevailing Wage in Virginia: Understanding Public Work Rules

If you plan to bid on public works projects in Virginia, you must understand prevailing wage laws. This is enforced by the [Virginia Department of Labor and Industry (DOLI)](https://www.doli.virginia.gov/labor-law/prevailing-wage-division/).

### Key Provisions and Thresholds

Effective July 1, 2021, Virginia significantly expanded its prevailing wage requirements.

- **State-Funded Projects:** All public works contracts entered into by any public body (state agency, city, county, school board, etc.) must include prevailing wage requirements if the contract value exceeds $250,000.

- **Wage Determinations:** DOLI issues prevailing wage rate schedules for various crafts and localities. These rates include both a basic hourly rate and fringe benefits. You must pay your workers no less than these rates.

- **Certified Payroll:** Contractors on prevailing wage projects must submit certified payroll records to DOLI (or the contracting public body) demonstrating compliance.

- **Federal Davis-Bacon Act:** For federally funded public works projects (e.g., federal highway projects, military construction), the federal Davis-Bacon Act applies, which has its own wage determinations and compliance requirements (administered by the U.S. Department of Labor).

Complying with prevailing wage can be complex. It requires meticulous record-keeping and often necessitates higher labor costs. Factor this into your bidding strategy for public work.

## Virginia Construction Startup FAQs

### Q: Do I need a Class A, B, or C license in Virginia?

A: It depends on the size of the projects you intend to undertake. A Class C license is for individual jobs up to $10,000 and total projects under $150,000 annually. A Class B is for jobs between $10,000 and $120,000, with annual totals under $7.5 million. A Class A license allows you to bid on and perform projects of unlimited value. You should apply for the highest class that your Designated Employee and Qualified Individual(s) qualify for based on their experience and your financial capacity, as this maximizes your opportunities.

### Q: How long does it take to get a contractor's license in Virginia?

A: The process can take anywhere from 2 to 4 months, sometimes longer if there are issues with your application.
