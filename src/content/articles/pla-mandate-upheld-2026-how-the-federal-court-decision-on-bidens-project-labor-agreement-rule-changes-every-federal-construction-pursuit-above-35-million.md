## What a PLA Actually Requires on Federal Construction Projects

A Project Labor Agreement is a pre-hire collective bargaining agreement that establishes terms and conditions of employment for all workers on a specific federal project, regardless of whether those workers are already union members. The PLA is negotiated before the first shovel hits the ground, covering wages, benefits, hours, grievance procedures, and trade work rules for every craft on the project site.

Under the upheld mandate, any federal [construction contract](/article/how-to-write-construction-contract-guide-2026/) valued at $35 million or above requires a PLA as a condition of contract award. Below that threshold, individual federal agencies retain discretion to require or waive PLAs based on project-specific factors. For practical purposes, if your federal pipeline includes any projects in the $35 million-plus range, PLA compliance is now a hard requirement, not a negotiating point with the contracting officer.

**What the PLA specifically requires from every prime contractor:**

- A signed pre-hire agreement with the relevant building trades unions before any workers are hired for the project

- Wage rates at or above union scale, which typically runs 5-15% above the Davis-Bacon prevailing wage already mandated on federal construction

- Contributions to union-affiliated benefit funds covering health insurance, pension, and apprenticeship training programs

- Union referral hall participation for a specified percentage of new craft worker hires, commonly 50% or more depending on the agreement

- Dues check-off provisions, though full union membership is not required of individual workers

- Project-specific grievance and arbitration procedures that govern all labor disputes on the project, independent of your standard contract dispute resolution mechanisms

For non-union contractors, the most important distinction is what the PLA does not require. It does not require you to permanently recognize a union, convert your company to a union shop, or commit your workforce to union membership on future projects. The PLA obligation is project-specific. Your [construction project management](/article/construction-project-management-surviving-the-messy-middle/) systems and labor policies revert to their current state the moment that federal project closes out. Understanding that boundary is what determines whether a non-union contractor treats this as a business opportunity or an existential threat.

Arbitration is the procedural mechanism that replaces your standard dispute resolution on PLA projects. Disputes between workers or unions and the contractor go to a project arbitrator, often within 48-72 hours of a grievance being filed. Arbitration decisions can favor unions on jurisdictional and work rule disputes. Contractors who run PLA projects without briefing their field supervision on grievance triggers will generate arbitration costs that erode the project margin faster than the labor premium itself.

## The Merit-Shop Pathway: Non-Union Contractors Can Still Win Federal Work

The PLA mandate does not lock non-union contractors out of federal work above $35 million. It changes the operating rules for the duration of a covered project. Understanding the merit-shop pathway is what separates contractors who voluntarily exit the federal market from those who adapt, price correctly, and compete.

The core principle is this: a PLA is a project-specific agreement, not a corporate-level union recognition. When you sign a PLA, you are agreeing to operate under union work rules and compensation standards for that project only. Your company does not become a union contractor. Your workers do not permanently join the union. When the project ends, the PLA expires and your standard labor practices resume without any ongoing union obligation.

**How the merit-shop pathway works in practice:**

- Before submitting a bid, engage a labor relations consultant or construction attorney experienced with PLA negotiations in your primary federal markets

- Identify which building trades unions hold jurisdiction over the work classifications on the specific project type and geography

- Negotiate project-specific PLA terms, including the ratio of union hall referrals to direct hires from your existing workforce

- Seek a "me-too" or "acknowledgment" agreement that allows you to sign onto an existing area-wide PLA rather than negotiating a standalone agreement from scratch, which reduces legal cost significantly

- Brief your existing workforce clearly: the PLA requires operating under union work rules for the project duration but does not require permanent union membership or ongoing dues obligation after the project

- Set up benefit fund remittances and dues check-off payroll procedures as part of your mobilization checklist, not as an afterthought after the first payroll run

Worker classification is the practical complexity that first-time PLA contractors underestimate most. Union work rules define jurisdiction by trade, which means your multi-trade self-perform workforce may be split across several union jurisdictions on a PLA project. A carpenter cannot perform ironworker work even if they are fully capable on your open-shop projects. A laborer cannot operate equipment controlled by an operating engineer local. Productivity planning must account for jurisdictional boundaries in the labor loading of every activity on your schedule.

The diversity dimension of federal contracting also intersects with PLA projects. Many federal agencies layer DBE, MBE, and WBE participation requirements on top of PLA compliance. Understanding how [women in construction](/article/women-in-construction-breaking-barriers-2026/) and minority-owned subcontractors are impacted by PLA flow-down requirements, and how to structure your sub roster to meet both PLA and diversity participation goals simultaneously, is a bid differentiator that most competitors are not thinking about yet.

## Subcontractor Flow-Down and the Federal Bid Strategy Shift for [Construction Business Growth](/article/how-to-scale-a-family-construction-business-without-losing-its-soul/) 2026

The PLA mandate does not stop at the prime contractor. Every subcontractor working on a covered federal project must sign onto the PLA before they can begin work on the project site. This flow-down requirement is where bids most often fall apart after award, because the prime contractor did not vet their sub roster for PLA readiness before submitting the bid.

Subcontractor flow-down creates a cascading compliance obligation through every tier of the subcontract chain. Your tier-one subs must sign the PLA. Their tier-two subs must sign it. Every employer with workers on the project site is covered, regardless of how far down the subcontract chain they sit. A non-compliant subcontractor discovered after mobilization is a project delay, a potential cure notice from the contracting officer, and a rebidding cost that easily exceeds $50,000 on a large federal project.

**Updating your subcontract templates for PLA flow-down compliance:**

- Add an explicit PLA acknowledgment clause requiring subs to sign onto the project PLA as a condition of their subcontract, not as a post-award negotiation

- Include representations from subs confirming their workforce can operate under PLA work rules and jurisdictional requirements for the relevant trades

- Add a termination-for-cause provision specifically for PLA non-compliance, with a cure period of no more than 5 business days to avoid project delay

- Require subs to provide proof of benefit fund enrollment and dues check-off setup before mobilization, not on the first pay date

- Update your standard lien waiver and compliance certification package to include PLA-specific attestations alongside your standard E-Verify and Davis-Bacon certifications

On the bid strategy side, federal pursuits above $35 million now require a pre-bid phase that most contractors are not running. Before submitting the bid, you need a signed PLA or signed letter of intent with the relevant trades, a labor coordinator identified and budgeted, and written confirmation from your major subcontractors that they will sign on. Bidding without this pre-work is not just risky -- it is a compliance exposure that can result in bid rejection before scoring begins.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does the PLA mandate apply to all federal construction contracts?

No. The upheld mandate applies specifically to federal construction contracts valued at $35 million or above. Below that threshold, individual federal agencies retain discretion to require or waive PLAs based on project-specific factors. However, contracts funded through IIJA pass-through to state agencies may trigger both federal and state PLA requirements simultaneously depending on the funding structure and the state receiving the funds.

### Can a non-union contractor win a federal project with a PLA requirement?

Yes. The merit-shop pathway allows non-union contractors to sign onto a project-specific PLA without permanently recognizing a union or converting their company to a union shop. Workers operate under union work rules for the project duration but do not become permanent union members. The PLA obligation expires at project closeout. An estimated 35-45% of PLA-covered federal projects are executed by merit-shop contractors who have built the necessary labor compliance infrastructure into their operations.

### How much will a PLA increase my labor costs on a federal bid?

Expect total labor cost increases of 8-18% on PLA-covered projects compared to prevailing wage open-shop work. PLA wages run 5-15% above Davis-Bacon prevailing wage, and union benefit fund contributions add 30-40% of wages in benefits on top of that. On a $40 million project with a 35% labor component, the total PLA labor premium ranges from $980,000 to $2.52 million depending on trade mix and local union benefit fund rates.

### What happens if one of my subcontractors refuses to sign the PLA?

A subcontractor who will not sign onto the project PLA cannot work on the covered project. Their subcontract must be terminated for cause and replaced with a PLA-compliant sub. Discovering a non-compliant sub after award generates project delays, potential cure notices from the contracting officer, and rebidding costs that routinely exceed $50,000 on large federal projects. This is why pre-bid vetting of your sub roster for PLA readiness is mandatory, not optional.

### Do PLA work rules affect productivity on federal projects?

Yes, and the impact must be planned for in your schedule and cost model. Union work rules define trade jurisdiction, meaning multi-trade self-perform work is split among separate craft classifications with no crossover. Contractors new to PLA execution typically see 10-15% productivity adjustments in the first project as field supervision adapts to jurisdictional boundaries. By the second and third PLA project, most crews reach or exceed open-shop productivity levels as supervisors internalize the work rules.
