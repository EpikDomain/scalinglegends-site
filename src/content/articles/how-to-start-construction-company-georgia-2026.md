## Laying the Foundation: Business Entity Formation in Georgia

Before you even think about swinging a hammer, you need to set up your business legally. This isn't just paperwork; it's about protecting yourself and your assets. The Georgia Secretary of State (SOS) in Atlanta is your primary point of contact for this.

### Choosing Your Business Structure: LLC vs. Corporation

The two most common choices for construction companies are a Limited Liability Company (LLC) or a Corporation (S-Corp or C-Corp). Each has pros and cons for liability, taxation, and administrative burden.

- **Limited Liability Company (LLC):** This is often the preferred choice for new contractors. It offers personal liability protection, meaning your personal assets are generally shielded from business debts and lawsuits. It also provides flexible taxation – you can be taxed as a sole proprietorship (pass-through), a partnership, or even an S-Corp or C-Corp. For a single-member LLC, profits and losses "pass through" to your personal tax return, avoiding double taxation.

- **S-Corporation (S-Corp):** An S-Corp also offers liability protection and pass-through taxation, similar to an LLC. The main difference is how owner compensation is handled; S-Corp owners must pay themselves a "reasonable salary" subject to payroll taxes (FICA), which can sometimes lead to tax savings compared to an LLC if structured correctly, especially as your business grows. It involves more formal corporate formalities.

- **C-Corporation (C-Corp):** This structure offers the strongest liability protection but is subject to "double taxation" – the corporation pays taxes on its profits, and then shareholders pay taxes again on dividends received. C-Corps are generally less common for small to medium construction companies unless you plan to raise significant capital from investors or go public down the line.

**Recommendation:** For most startups, an LLC is the simplest and most flexible option. As your company grows and profits increase, consult with a Georgia-based CPA to see if converting to an S-Corp would offer tax advantages.

### Registering Your Business with the Georgia Secretary of State

- **Choose a Business Name:** Ensure your desired name is available by checking the Georgia SOS business name database. It must be distinguishable from other registered entities.

- **Appoint a Registered Agent:** You'll need a registered agent with a physical street address in Georgia (not a P.O. Box) who can receive legal documents on behalf of your company. This can be you, another employee, or a professional registered agent service.

**File Formation Documents:**
        

            - For an LLC: File "Articles of Organization" with the Georgia Secretary of State.

            - For a Corporation: File "Articles of Incorporation" with the Georgia Secretary of State.

        

        You can do this online through the Georgia SOS website (www.sos.ga.gov) or by mail. The filing fee is typically around $100 for initial registration.

    
- **Obtain an Employer Identification Number (EIN):** Once your business entity is formed, you'll need an EIN from the IRS. This is like a Social Security Number for your business and is required for opening bank accounts, hiring employees, and filing taxes. It's free and can be obtained instantly online.

- **Draft an Operating Agreement (LLC) or Bylaws (Corporation):** While not filed with the state, these internal documents are crucial. An Operating Agreement for an LLC outlines ownership percentages, management structure, and how decisions are made. Bylaws for a Corporation do the same. Get these done by an attorney; they protect you and your partners.

### Georgia Tax Implications for Your Business Entity

**State Income Tax:** Georgia has a flat state income tax rate (currently 5.49% for 2026, though it's been trending down slightly).
        

            - LLCs taxed as pass-through entities (sole prop/partnership) don't pay state income tax at the entity level; owners pay it on their personal returns.

            - S-Corps also pass profits/losses through to owners, who pay state income tax personally.

            - C-Corps pay state corporate income tax on their profits.

        

    
- **Annual Registration:** All Georgia corporations and LLCs must file an annual registration with the Georgia SOS and pay a fee (currently $50) to maintain their active status. This is not a "franchise tax" in the traditional sense, but it is an annual state obligation.

## Georgia Contractor Licensing: Your Non-Negotiable Requirement

This is where many aspiring contractors stumble. Georgia takes licensing seriously, and for good reason. You cannot legally bid on or perform most construction projects in the state without the appropriate license from the Georgia State Board of Residential and General Contractors, which operates under the Georgia Division of Professional Licensing (DPL). Don't even think about cutting corners here.

### Understanding Georgia's License Classes

The license you need depends heavily on the type and size of projects you intend to undertake. Here are the main classifications:

**Residential Basic Contractor:**
        

            - **Scope:** Permits construction, alteration, repair, or demolition of one-family and two-family residences and accessory structures, provided the project is not more than three stories in height and contains not more than four units. Excludes multi-family structures (more than two units).

            - **Financial Requirement:** Must demonstrate a minimum net worth of $20,000 OR provide a $20,000 surety bond.

            - **Experience:** At least two years of documented experience in residential construction.

        

    
**Residential Light Commercial Contractor:**
        

            - **Scope:** Allows all work permitted by the Residential Basic license, PLUS construction, alteration, repair, or demolition of commercial structures and multi-family dwellings not exceeding four stories in height and 25,000 square feet.

            - **Financial Requirement:** Must demonstrate a minimum net worth of $150,000 OR provide a $150,000 surety bond.

            - **Experience:** At least two years of documented experience in residential or light commercial construction.

        

    
**General Contractor:**
        

            - **Scope:** The big one. This license allows you to undertake any type of construction project, regardless of type, size, or cost, including commercial, industrial, public works, and residential projects of any scale. There are NO project value limits.

            - **Financial Requirement:** Must demonstrate a minimum net worth of $150,000 OR provide a $150,000 surety bond.

            - **Experience:** At least three years of documented experience in general construction, with at least one year as a superintendent or project manager overseeing major trades.

        

    
**General Contractor Restricted:**
        

            - **Scope:** Similar to a General Contractor, but typically restricted to projects with a contract value limit (e.g., $1,000,000 or $500,000). This license is less common for new applicants as the General Contractor license is often preferred if the experience is there.

            - **Financial Requirement:** Varies depending on the restriction, but typically lower than the full GC.

            - **Experience:** Similar to General Contractor, but potentially less stringent depending on the restriction.

        

    
**Utility Contractor:**
        

            - **Scope:** Focuses on construction, repair, or improvement of utility systems (water, sewer, storm drainage).

            - **Financial Requirement:** Minimum net worth of $20,000 OR a $20,000 surety bond.

            - **Experience:** At least two years of documented experience in utility construction.

        

    

### The Licensing Process Through Georgia DPL

- **Meet Experience Requirements:** Gather documentation (W2s, tax returns, project lists, letters from employers/clients) to prove your required years of experience.

- **Meet Financial Requirements:** Prepare your financial statements (balance sheet) or secure a surety bond from a licensed bond provider.

- **Complete the Application:** Download the specific application for your desired license from the Georgia DPL website (www.sos.ga.gov/licensing/plb/21). Fill it out accurately and completely.

**Pass the Exam:** All Georgia contractor licenses require passing a state-administered exam covering business and law, and a trade-specific portion. These exams are typically administered by PSI.
        

            - **Exam Content:** The Business and Law exam covers topics like estimating, contracts, project management, lien laws, safety, and financial management. The trade exam focuses on specific construction knowledge relevant to your license class.

            - **Preparation:** Don't wing it. Invest in study guides, practice tests, and consider an exam prep course from a reputable provider.

        

    
- **Submit Application and Fees:** Once you pass the exams, submit your completed application, all supporting documentation, and the non-refundable application fee (typically $150-$200) to the Georgia DPL.

- **Background Check:** Be prepared for a criminal background check as part of the application process.

- **Board Review:** Your application will be reviewed by the Georgia State Board of Residential and General Contractors. This can take several weeks or even months. Be patient and responsive to any requests for additional information.

**Pro Tip:** Start the application and experience documentation process well before you plan to take the exam. It's often the most time-consuming part.

## Essential Georgia-Specific Insurance and Bonding Requirements

Operating a construction company without proper insurance in Georgia is like building a house without a foundation – it’s just asking for trouble. And for certain projects, bonding is non-negotiable.

### General Liability Insurance

This is your primary defense against claims of property damage, bodily injury, and advertising injury caused by your business operations. While Georgia doesn't have a specific state-mandated minimum for all contractors, virtually every client, general contractor, and municipality will require you to carry it. A common minimum is $1,000,000 per occurrence / $2,000,000 aggregate. Your policy should also include "Completed Operations" coverage, which protects you after the project is finished.

### Workers' Compensation Insurance

This is mandated by Georgia law. If you have three or more employees (full-time or part-time), you are legally required to carry Workers' Compensation insurance. This covers medical expenses and lost wages for employees injured on the job. The State Board of Workers' Compensation (SBWC) oversees these requirements. Costs vary significantly based on your payroll, the risk classification of your work (e.g., roofing is riskier than painting), and your company's claims history (Experience Modification Rate or EMR).

**Important:** Even if you're a sole proprietor or have fewer than three employees, carrying Workers' Comp can be a smart move, as it protects you and your team from potentially devastating medical costs and lawsuits.

### Commercial Auto Insurance

If you use vehicles for business purposes – trucks, vans, trailers – you need commercial auto insurance. Personal auto policies typically won't cover business-related accidents. Georgia mandates minimum liability coverage for all vehicles, but your commercial policy should have significantly higher limits.

### Surety Bonds

Bonds are not insurance, but a guarantee. They assure your client that you will complete the work according to contract and pay your subcontractors and suppliers. They are critical for securing larger projects, especially public works.

- **Bid Bonds:** Guarantee that you will enter into the contract if your bid is accepted.

- **Performance Bonds:** Guarantee that you will complete the project according to the contract terms and specifications.

- **Payment Bonds:** Guarantee that you will pay your subcontractors and suppliers.

For most public projects in Georgia (state, county, municipal), payment and performance bonds are required for projects exceeding a certain threshold (often $100,000 or more, depending on the entity). Getting bonded requires a strong financial history, a good credit score, and a solid business plan. Start building a relationship with a surety bond agent early.

## Navigating Georgia's Tax Obligations

Taxes are a reality of doing business. Understanding Georgia's specific tax structure will save you headaches and penalties.

### State Income Tax

As mentioned, Georgia has a flat state income tax rate (currently 5.49% for 2026, subject to legislative changes).

- If your business is a pass-through entity (LLC, S-Corp), you'll pay this on your personal income tax return (Form 500) based on your share of the business profits.

- If you're a C-Corporation, your business will pay corporate income tax on its profits (Form 600).

You'll likely need to make estimated tax payments throughout the year to avoid penalties.

### Sales Tax on Materials

This is a big one for contractors. In Georgia, the state sales tax rate is 4%. However, counties and cities can impose additional local option sales taxes (LOST). This means the combined sales tax rate can range from 4% to 9% depending on where the materials are purchased and/or where the project is located (check the specific county/city rates).

- **Who Pays?** Generally, contractors are considered the "end users" of materials incorporated into real property. This means you typically pay sales tax on materials when you purchase them from your suppliers. You do NOT then charge sales tax to your client on the labor or on the materials that become part of the real property.

- **Repair vs. New Construction:** There can be nuances depending on whether it's new construction, repair, or renovation, and whether you're acting as a contractor or a retailer. Consult with a Georgia tax professional to ensure you're compliant.

### Employer Withholding Taxes

If you have employees, you'll need to withhold Georgia income tax from their wages and remit it to the Georgia Department of Revenue (DOR), along with federal withholding taxes to the IRS.

### Unemployment Insurance Tax

As an employer, you'll pay state unemployment insurance taxes to the Georgia Department of Labor (DOL). Your rate will depend on your industry and claims history.

### Local Business Licenses and Occupational Taxes

Beyond state requirements, most cities and counties in Georgia require you to obtain a local business license and pay an occupational tax (sometimes called a business license fee) to operate within their jurisdiction. The fees and requirements vary widely. Always check with the city hall and county government where you plan to operate.

### Government Opportunities

Public projects are a steady source of work, but they come with specific requirements.

- **Georgia Department of Transportation (GDOT):** Bids for road, bridge, and infrastructure projects. Register as a vendor on their website.

- **Georgia State Financing and Investment Commission (GSFIC):** Oversees construction for state buildings (universities, prisons, government offices).

- **Local Municipalities & Counties:** Every city and county (e.g., City of Atlanta, Fulton County, Gwinnett County) issues RFPs (Request for Proposals) or ITBs (Invitation to Bid) for public buildings, parks, infrastructure, and maintenance. Check their procurement websites.

- **School Boards:** Local school districts regularly bid out projects for new schools, additions, and renovations.

**Georgia Prevailing Wage Requirements:** This is a critical point. Unlike many states, Georgia DOES NOT have a state-level prevailing wage law for state-funded construction projects. This means you are not legally required to pay union wages or specific "prevailing" rates on projects funded solely by the State of Georgia or its subdivisions. HOWEVER, if a project receives ANY federal funding, then federal prevailing wage laws (specifically the Davis-Bacon Act) WILL apply, requiring you to pay federal prevailing wages and benefits. Always check project specifications for federal funding.

- **Subcontracting:** Many new companies start as subcontractors for larger GCs. This is an excellent way to gain experience, build relationships, and get your foot in the door.

- **Direct Client Work:** For residential, renovation, or smaller commercial projects, you can market directly to homeowners, businesses, and property managers.

- **Networking:** Join industry associations like AGC Georgia (Associated General Contractors of Georgia), ABC of Georgia (Associated Builders and Contractors of Georgia), and local chambers of commerce. Attend their events. Relationships are king in Georgia construction.

## Georgia Minority/Disadvantaged Business Certifications (MBE/DBE)

If your company is owned and controlled by individuals who are part of certain disadvantaged groups, you may qualify for state and local certifications that can open doors to specific contract opportunities.

- **Disadvantaged Business Enterprise (DBE):** This is a federal program administered by state agencies, such as the Georgia Department of Transportation (GDOT). If you're certified as a DBE, you can bid on federally funded transportation projects where a percentage of the work is set aside for DBEs.

- **Minority Business Enterprise (MBE) / Women Business Enterprise (WBE):** Many cities and counties (e.g., City of Atlanta, Fulton County, MARTA) have their own M/WBE programs. These programs aim to promote diversity in contracting by setting participation goals for certified minority- and women-owned businesses on their projects.

**Benefits:** These certifications can provide a competitive edge, access to set-aside contracts, and networking opportunities. The application process is rigorous, requiring proof of ownership, control, and personal net worth. Research the specific requirements of the certifying agency (GDOT, City of Atlanta, etc.).

## Frequently Asked Questions About Starting a Construction Company in Georgia

### Do I need a license to do handyman work in Georgia?

Generally, no. Georgia's state contractor licensing laws apply to projects exceeding certain thresholds or specific types of construction. For minor repairs or projects under a certain value (often $2,500, but this can vary by local ordinance and the nature of the work), a state contractor license is typically not required. However, you will still need a local business license in the city and/or county where you operate, and you must comply with all local building codes and permitting requirements for any structural, electrical, or plumbing work.

### What is the minimum net worth requirement for a Georgia General Contractor license?

For a full General Contractor license in Georgia, you must demonstrate a minimum net worth of $150,000. Alternatively, you can provide a $150,000 surety bond in lieu of demonstrating the full net worth. The Residential Basic license requires $20,000 net worth or bond, and the Residential Light Commercial license requires $150,000 net worth or bond.

### Does Georgia have reciprocity with other states for contractor licenses?

Georgia does have limited reciprocity agreements for contractor licenses with some states, but it's not universal. The Georgia Board of Residential and General Contractors reviews reciprocity applications on a case-by-case basis. Generally, the requirements of the other state's license must be substantially similar or more stringent than Georgia's. You'll still need to complete a Georgia-specific application and may need to pass the Georgia Business and Law exam. Always check the Georgia DPL website or contact them directly for the most current reciprocity agreements and requirements.

### How long does it take to get a contractor's license in Georgia?

The timeline can vary significantly. Once you've met all experience and financial requirements and passed the exams, the application processing time by the Georgia Division of Professional Licensing can take anywhere from 4-8 weeks, or even longer if there are issues with your application or during peak periods. It's best to allow several months from when you start preparing to when you expect to receive your license.

### Are subcontractors required to be licensed in Georgia?

It depends on the scope of work. If a subcontractor is performing work that falls under one of the state-licensed trades (e.g., plumbing, electrical, HVAC, conditioned air, low-voltage), they must hold the appropriate specialty license. General construction subcontractors (e.g., framing, drywall, roofing) typically do not need a state general contractor license if they are working under a properly licensed general contractor who is pulling the overall building permit. However, local jurisdictions may still require them to have a local business license. Always verify specific requirements based on the project and location.
