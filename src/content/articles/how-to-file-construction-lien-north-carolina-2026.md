A properly filed construction lien, also known as a mechanics lien, is a powerful tool to protect your right to payment. It places a security interest on the improved property itself, creating leverage that can be crucial when disputes arise.

This comprehensive guide, specifically tailored to North Carolina General Statutes §44A, cuts through the noise to provide you with a direct, no-BS explanation of how to navigate the lien process in the Tar Heel State. ## Understanding North Carolina Mechanics Lien Law

North Carolina's construction lien laws are primarily governed by [Chapter 44A of the North Carolina General Statutes](https://www.ncleg.gov/Laws/GeneralStatuteSections/Chapter44A). This statute outlines the rights and responsibilities of parties involved in [construction projects](/article/surviving-the-messy-middle-of-construction-growth), ensuring that those who furnish labor, materials, or services for the improvement of real property have a mechanism to secure payment.

### The Purpose of NC Construction Liens

A mechanics lien serves as a legal encumbrance on real property, providing a claimant with a security interest in the property itself. This means that if you are not paid for your work, you may have the right to force the sale of the property to satisfy your debt. This potential for foreclosure is what gives a lien its significant leverage, often prompting property owners or higher-tier contractors to resolve payment disputes quickly.

It's crucial to understand that a construction lien is distinct from a breach of contract claim. While both relate to non-payment, a lien provides a specific remedy against the improved property, whereas a contract claim is typically a personal claim against the party with whom you contracted.

### Who Has Lien Rights in North Carolina?

North Carolina law broadly grants lien rights to a variety of parties who contribute to the improvement of real property. This includes:

- **General Contractors:** [A general contractor](/article/how-to-get-general-contractor-license-florida-2026) (GC) has a direct lien against the real property if they have a contract directly with the owner. For work exceeding $30,000, North Carolina law requires general contractors to be licensed. An unlicensed contractor performing work above this threshold may face significant hurdles in enforcing a lien.

- **Subcontractors:** Subcontractors who contract with a general contractor or a higher-tier subcontractor have "derived" lien rights. Their lien rights are derived from the general contractor's lien rights. This means that if the general contractor has no lien rights (e.g., due to being paid in full), the subcontractors may also lose their ability to file a lien on the real property, though they may still have a lien upon funds.

- **Suppliers:** Material suppliers who furnish materials to a general contractor or a subcontractor are also entitled to lien rights.

- **Laborers:** Individuals who provide labor on a construction project.

- **Design Professionals:** Architects, engineers, and surveyors also have lien rights if they have a contract with the owner or a general contractor for the improvement of the property.

It's important to note that North Carolina law provides for lien rights for 1st, 2nd, and 3rd tier subcontractors and suppliers. However, the further down the contractual chain you are, the more complex the notice requirements become, particularly regarding the "Notice of Subcontract" and "Notice of Claim of Lien Upon Funds."

## Critical Preliminary Notices in North Carolina

North Carolina's lien law places a significant emphasis on preliminary notices, especially for parties not in direct contract with the property owner. Failing to send these notices correctly and on time can extinguish your lien rights entirely.

### Notice of Subcontract (NOC)

The Notice of Subcontract (NOC) is a crucial document for subcontractors and suppliers who are not in direct privity of contract with the property owner. Its primary purpose is to inform the owner that you are furnishing labor or materials to the project and to preserve your potential lien rights against the real property.

- **Who Needs to Send It:** All 1st, 2nd, and 3rd tier subcontractors and suppliers must serve a Notice of Subcontract. General contractors, having a direct contract with the owner, are exempt from this requirement.

- **Purpose:** To preserve the claimant's right to a lien on the real property. Without a properly served NOC, a subcontractor or supplier may lose their ability to file a Claim of Lien on Real Property if the owner has paid the general contractor in full.

- **Deadline:** The Notice of Subcontract must be served upon the owner within 5 business days of the claimant's first furnishing of labor or materials to the project. This is a strict deadline, and missing it can be fatal to your lien rights on the real property.

- **Service Method:** The NOC must be served by certified mail, return receipt requested, or by any other means authorized by Rule 4 of the North Carolina Rules of Civil Procedure (e.g., personal delivery by sheriff). Keep meticulous records of service.

- **Content Requirements:** The NOC must contain the name and address of the claimant, a general description of the labor or materials furnished, the name of the person with whom the claimant contracted, and a description of the project property.

### Notice of Claim of Lien Upon Funds (COLUF)

The Notice of Claim of Lien Upon Funds (COLUF) is another powerful tool under NC General Statutes §44A-18. Unlike a Claim of Lien on Real Property, which attaches to the physical property, a COLUF attaches to the funds owed from a higher-tier contractor to a lower-tier contractor or from the owner to the general contractor.

- **Who Needs to Send It:** All parties except the general contractor can utilize a COLUF. This includes subcontractors, suppliers, and laborers.

- **Purpose:** The COLUF acts as a stop notice, intercepting funds due to a higher-tier party. Once properly served, the party receiving the notice (e.g., the owner or GC) must withhold funds up to the amount of the lien claim from any payments due to the party against whom the lien is asserted. This effectively "freezes" the funds, preventing them from being disbursed further down the chain.

- **Deadline:** A COLUF can be served at any time after payment becomes due and owing, but critically, it must be served before the funds are disbursed by the obligor (the party who owes the funds). The sooner it's served, the more likely you are to intercept funds. It's often served concurrently with or prior to a Claim of Lien on Real Property.

- **Service Method:** Must be served by certified mail, return receipt requested, or by any other means authorized by Rule 4 of the North Carolina Rules of Civil Procedure. It must be served on the owner, the general contractor, and any higher-tier subcontractors in the contractual chain above the claimant.

- **Content Requirements:** The COLUF must state the name and address of the claimant, the name and address of the party with whom the claimant contracted, a description of the contract, a description of the labor or materials furnished, the amount claimed, and a description of the project property.

## Step-by-Step Guide: Filing a Claim of Lien on Real Property in North Carolina

Filing a Claim of Lien on Real Property is a precise legal process that must adhere strictly to North Carolina statutes. Follow these steps carefully to ensure your lien is valid and enforceable.

### Step 1: Verify Your Lien Rights and Project Eligibility

Before you begin, confirm that you meet the criteria for filing a lien in North Carolina:

- **Private Projects Only:** Mechanics liens in NC apply exclusively to private construction projects. Public projects require bond claims (discussed later).

- **Qualified Claimant:** Ensure you fall into one of the categories of parties with lien rights (GC, sub, supplier, laborer, design professional).

- **Contractor Licensing:** If you are a general contractor performing work over $30,000, verify that you hold a valid NC general contractor's license. Unlicensed work can invalidate your lien.

- **Preliminary Notice Compliance:** If you are a subcontractor or supplier, confirm that you properly served your Notice of Subcontract within 5 business days of first furnishing. If you failed to do so, your lien rights on the real property may be severely limited or lost, especially if the owner has paid the general contractor.

### Step 2: Gather Required Information for Your Lien Claim

Accuracy is paramount. Collect all necessary information before drafting your lien document:

- **Property Owner's Information:** Full legal name and current mailing address of the property owner(s).

- **Legal Description of Property:** A precise legal description of the real property, sufficient for identification. This can typically be found in the deed, tax records, or project documents. A street address alone is often insufficient.

- **Claimant's Information:** Your full legal name (company name), business address, and contact information.

- **Contractor's Information (if applicable):** Name and address of the general contractor or the specific subcontractor with whom you contracted.

- **Amount Claimed:** The exact dollar amount due and owing for the labor, materials, or services furnished to the property. This should only include the value of your contribution to the improvement, not speculative damages or attorney's fees.

- **Dates of Furnishing:** The date you first furnished labor or materials to the project and the date you last furnished labor or materials. The "last furnishing" date is critical for calculating deadlines.

- **Contract Details:** Reference to the contract under which you performed the work, including the date of the contract.

### Step 3: Prepare the Claim of Lien on Real Property Document

North Carolina law specifies the required contents for a Claim of Lien on Real Property under NC General Statutes §44A-12. While no specific statutory form exists, your document must include:

- The name and address of the person claiming the lien.

- The name and address of the record owner of the real property at the time the claim of lien is filed.

- A description of the real property sufficient to identify it, including the name of the county in which the real property is located.

- The name and address of the person with whom the claimant contracted.

- The date upon which labor or materials were first furnished upon the real property by the claimant.

- The date upon which labor or materials were last furnished upon the real property by the claimant.

- A brief description of the labor or materials furnished.

- The amount claimed.

- **Verification:** The lien claim must be signed by the claimant or their agent and verified (notarized).

Ensure the document is clearly titled "Claim of Lien on Real Property."

### Step 4: Record the Claim of Lien with the Clerk of Superior Court

This is the formal act of filing your lien and making it a matter of public record.

- **Where to File:** The Claim of Lien must be filed with the Clerk of Superior Court in the county where the real property is located. If the property spans multiple counties, you may need to file in each relevant county.

- **Deadline:** The Claim of Lien on Real Property must be filed within 120 days after the date upon which the claimant last furnished labor or materials at the site of the improvement. This 120-day period is absolute and cannot be extended.

- **Filing Fees:** There will be a filing fee associated with recording the lien. These fees vary by county, so it's advisable to check with the specific Clerk of Superior Court's office beforehand.

### Step 5: Serve the Recorded Claim of Lien on the Property Owner

After recording, you must notify the property owner of the lien.

- **Who to Serve:** The recorded Claim of Lien must be served on the owner of the property.

- **Deadline:** Service on the owner must also be completed within 120 days after the date upon which the claimant last furnished labor or materials. This means both recording and serving must occur within the same 120-day window.

- **Service Method:** Service must be made by personal delivery (e.g., via sheriff's deputy) or by certified mail, return receipt requested. Keep the return receipt as proof of service.

- **Proof of Service:** It is critical to retain proof that the lien was properly served. This documentation will be essential if the lien needs to be enforced later.

## Critical North Carolina Construction Lien Deadlines and Timeline

Mastering North Carolina's lien deadlines is non-negotiable. Missing even one can render your lien rights worthless. Here’s a summary of the critical timeline:

### North Carolina Lien Timeline Overview

- **Notice of Subcontract (for 1st, 2nd, 3rd tier subs/suppliers):** Served upon the owner within 5 business days of first furnishing labor or materials.

- **Notice of Claim of Lien Upon Funds (for all except GCs):** Can be served at any time after payment is due, but before funds are disbursed by the obligor (owner, GC, etc.). The sooner, the better, to intercept funds.

- **Claim of Lien on Real Property (for all claimants):** Must be filed with the Clerk of Superior Court within 120 days after the date upon which the claimant last furnished labor or materials.

- **Service of Claim of Lien on Real Property (for all claimants):** Must be served on the property owner within 120 days after the date upon which the claimant last furnished labor or materials.

- **Action to Enforce Lien (foreclosure lawsuit):** A lawsuit to enforce (foreclose) the Claim of Lien on Real Property must be filed in Superior Court within 180 days after the date upon which the claimant last furnished labor or materials.

### Calculating "Last Furnishing"

The "last furnishing" date is the linchpin for most lien deadlines in North Carolina. This date refers to the last day you performed actual, lienable work or delivered materials for the project. Be cautious about what you consider "last furnishing":

- **What Counts:** Typically, the performance of contract work, delivery of essential materials, or substantial completion activities.

- **What May Not Count:** Minor punch list work, warranty repairs, or the removal of equipment after the main work is completed generally do not extend the "last furnishing" date. The work must be part of the original contract or a change order that substantially contributes to the improvement.

If there's any ambiguity, it's safer to assume an earlier "last furnishing" date to avoid missing a deadline.

## Enforcing Your North Carolina Construction Lien

Recording and serving a Claim of Lien on Real Property is only the first step. To realize the full benefit of your lien, you must enforce it through a legal action.

### Filing a Lawsuit to Enforce the Lien

If your lien claim remains unpaid after proper filing and service, the next step is to initiate a lawsuit to enforce (or "foreclose") the lien. This is a judicial process to compel the sale of the property to satisfy your debt.

- **Deadline:** The lawsuit to enforce the Claim of Lien on Real Property must be filed in the Superior Court of the county where the property is located within 180 days after the date upon which the claimant last furnished labor or materials. This 180-day deadline is absolute. If you miss it, your lien expires and becomes unenforceable.

- **Venue:** The lawsuit must be filed in the Superior Court of the county where the real property is located.

- **Lis Pendens:** Concurrently with filing the lawsuit, you must also file a notice of [*lis pendens*](https://www.ncleg.gov/EnactedLegislation/Statutes/HTML/BySection/Chapter_1-A/GS_1-232.html) (Latin for "suit pending") with the Clerk of Superior Court in the same county. This notice serves as a public record that a lawsuit affecting the title to the property is ongoing, warning potential purchasers or lenders. Failure to file a *lis pendens* can render a judgment enforcing the lien ineffective against subsequent purchasers or encumbrancers.

### Foreclosure Process

If your lawsuit is successful, the court will typically enter a judgment declaring your lien valid and ordering the sale of the property to satisfy your claim. The proceeds from the sale are then distributed according to the priority of liens. This process can be lengthy and complex, often requiring significant legal representation.

## North Carolina Lien Waivers: What You Need to Know

Lien waivers are common documents in North Carolina construction projects, used to release or waive lien rights in exchange for payment. Understanding their proper use and limitations is crucial.

### Validity of Lien Waivers in North Carolina

North Carolina law generally recognizes and enforces lien waivers. However, there are specific considerations:

**Conditional vs. Unconditional Waivers:**

- **Conditional Waiver:** This type of waiver becomes effective *only upon actual receipt of payment*. It's typically used for progress payments, stating that the waiver is effective once the specified payment has cleared. For example, "This waiver is effective upon receipt of $X payment."

- **Unconditional Waiver:** This waiver is effective immediately upon signing, regardless of whether payment has actually been received. You should generally avoid signing unconditional waivers for progress payments unless you have already received and confirmed the funds.

- **Best Practice:** Always use conditional waivers for progress payments. Only provide an unconditional waiver after you have verified that the payment has cleared your bank.

- **Final Payment Waivers:** For final payment, an unconditional waiver is often required. Ensure that the final payment amount covers all outstanding balances before signing.

### Important NC Statute on Waivers

North Carolina law specifically prohibits certain types of lien waiver clauses. [NC General Statutes §22B-2](https://www.ncleg.gov/EnactedLegislation/Statutes/HTML/BySection/Chapter_22B/GS_22B-2.html) states that any provision in a [construction contract that](/article/how-to-write-construction-contract-guide-2026) purports to waive or release the right to file a claim of lien on real property or a claim of lien upon funds *before the furnishing of labor or materials* is against public policy and void. This means "no-lien clauses" in contracts that attempt to waive future lien rights are unenforceable. Waivers are only valid for work already performed and for which payment is being exchanged.

## Bond Claims on North Carolina Public Projects (NC Little Miller Act)

Mechanics liens cannot be filed against public property (e.g., schools, government buildings, public roads). To protect subcontractors and suppliers on these projects, North Carolina has its own version of the federal Miller Act, commonly referred to as the "Little Miller Act," found in [NC General Statutes §44A-25 through §44A-32](https://www.ncleg.gov/EnactedLegislation/Statutes/HTML/BySection/Chapter_44A/GS_44A-25.html).

### Understanding Public Project Security

Instead of a lien on the property, public projects require the general contractor to furnish payment bonds. These bonds guarantee that subcontractors and suppliers will be paid, providing a substitute for the security offered by a mechanics lien.

### Who Can Make a Bond Claim?

Subcontractors, suppliers, and laborers who furnish labor or materials to a general contractor or a direct subcontractor on a public project are generally protected by the payment bond.

### Key Deadlines for NC Bond Claims

The deadlines for bond claims are distinct from mechanics liens:

- **Notice of Claim to General Contractor:** If you do not have a direct contractual relationship with the general contractor (i.e., you are a sub-subcontractor or a supplier to a subcontractor), you must give written notice of your claim to the general contractor. This notice must be given within 120 days after the date on which you last furnished labor or materials for which payment is claimed.

- **Filing Suit on Bond:** An action on the payment bond must be instituted within one year after the day on which the last of the labor was performed or material was furnished by the claimant.

### Required Content of Bond Claim Notice

The notice to the general contractor should state with substantial accuracy the amount claimed, the name of the party to whom the materials were furnished or for whom the labor was performed, and the project on which the work was performed.

## Common Mistakes That Invalidate North Carolina Construction Liens

North Carolina's lien laws are unforgiving. A single misstep can render your lien claim unenforceable. Be vigilant and avoid these common pitfalls:

- **Missing Deadlines:** This is the most frequent and fatal error. Whether it's the 5-business-day Notice of Subcontract, the 120-day filing/service deadline for the Claim of Lien, or the 180-day enforcement lawsuit deadline, missing any of these will likely extinguish your rights.

- **Incorrect Property Description:** Using an insufficient or inaccurate legal description of the property is a common error. A street address alone is rarely enough; you need a proper legal description.

- **Failing to Serve Notices Correctly:** Improper service (e.g., regular mail instead of certified mail, serving the wrong party) can invalidate your lien. Always retain proof of service.

- **Claiming Excessive Amounts:** Liening for more than the reasonable value of labor/materials furnished can lead to challenges and even penalties. Only include the actual amount due for lienable work.

- **Not Verifying the Lien Claim:** The Claim of Lien on Real Property must be signed and notarized (verified). Failing to do so makes it invalid.

- **Failing to File a Notice of Subcontract:** For subcontractors and suppliers, neglecting to serve the NOC within 5 business days can result in the loss of your lien rights against the real property if the owner has fully paid the general contractor.

- **Forgetting to File a Lawsuit to Enforce the Lien:** A recorded lien is merely a placeholder. It must be actively enforced through a lawsuit within 180 days of last furnishing, along with a [*lis pendens*](https://www.ncleg.gov/EnactedLegislation/Statutes/HTML/BySection/Chapter_1-A/GS_1-232.html), or it expires.

- **Unlicensed Contractor (for GCs):** Performing general contracting work over $30,000 without a valid NC license can prevent you from enforcing a mechanics lien.

## Releasing and Discharging a North Carolina Lien

Once your payment dispute is resolved, or your lien claim is satisfied, you have a legal obligation to release or discharge the lien from the property record.

### When to Release a Lien

A lien should be released promptly when:

- You have received full payment for the amount claimed in the lien.

- The dispute has been settled to your satisfaction.

- A court has ordered the lien discharged.

### The Release Document

To release a lien, you must prepare and record a "Satisfaction of Claim of Lien" or "Lien Release" document. This document should:

- Clearly identify the original Claim of Lien on Real Property by its recording information (book and page number, or instrument number).

- State that the lien has been satisfied and is hereby released.

- Be signed by the claimant or their authorized agent.

- Be notarized.

This release document must then be recorded with the same Clerk of Superior Court where the original lien was filed. Recording the release removes the encumbrance from the property's title.

### Failure to Release

North Carolina law provides for penalties if a lien claimant fails to release a satisfied lien within a reasonable time after being requested to do so. Under [NC General Statutes §44A-16](https://www.ncleg.gov/EnactedLegislation/Statutes/HTML/BySection/Chapter_44A/GS_44A-16.html), an owner may recover damages if a lien is not canceled after payment. Promptly releasing a lien upon satisfaction is not just good practice, it's a legal requirement.

## North Carolina-Specific Filing Fees and Costs

While the exact fees can vary slightly by county, here's a general overview of costs associated with the North Carolina lien process:

- **Recording Fees:** The Clerk of Superior Court charges a fee for recording the Claim of Lien on Real Property and for recording the subsequent lien release. These fees are typically in the range of $20-$30 per document. It's best to call the specific county's Clerk of Superior Court office for current rates.

**Service Fees:**

- **Certified Mail:** The cost of sending documents via certified mail with return receipt requested (e.g., Notice of Subcontract, Claim of Lien service) is typically around $8-$10 per mailing.

- **Sheriff's Service:** If you opt for personal service by a sheriff's deputy, there will be a service fee, generally in the range of $30-$50 per attempt.

- **Notarization Fees:** You'll incur small fees for notarizing your lien claim and release documents.

- **Attorney Fees:** If you engage a North Carolina construction attorney to draft, file, serve, or enforce your lien, these will be the most significant costs. While some statutes allow for recovery of attorney fees in enforcement actions, it's not guaranteed.

Budgeting for these costs is an important part of the lien process. Factor them into your overall strategy for securing payment.

## Frequently Asked Questions About North Carolina Construction Liens

### Q: Can a North Carolina lien be filed on a residential property?

A: Yes, North Carolina's mechanics lien laws apply to both commercial and residential private properties. However, there can be additional considerations for owner-occupied dwellings, particularly regarding the Notice of Claim of Lien Upon Funds, as the owner may be less likely to be withholding funds from a general contractor on a smaller residential project.

### Q: What is the difference between a Claim of Lien on Real Property and a Claim of Lien Upon Funds in North Carolina?

A: A **Claim of Lien on Real Property** (NCGS §44A-8) attaches to the actual land and buildings, providing a security interest in the property itself. A **Claim of Lien Upon Funds** (NCGS §44A-18) does not attach to the property but rather to the money owed from a higher-tier party (e.g., owner, general contractor) to a lower-tier party in the contractual chain. The COLUF serves to "freeze" funds, preventing them from being disbursed until the claim is resolved.

### Q: Do I need a written contract to file a North Carolina mechanics lien?

A: While North Carolina statutes do not explicitly require a written contract for a valid mechanics lien, having a clear, written agreement for your labor or materials makes proving the existence and terms of your contract, and thus your lien claim, significantly easier. It is always highly recommended to have a written contract.

### Q: What happens if I miss a North Carolina lien deadline?

A: Missing any of the critical deadlines (e.g., Notice of Subcontract, 120-day filing/service, 180-day enforcement lawsuit) is usually fatal to your lien rights. North Carolina courts strictly interpret these statutory requirements, and there are generally no exceptions for late filings or service.

### Q: Can I include attorney's fees or lost profits in my North Carolina lien amount?

A: Generally, no. The amount claimed in a North Carolina Claim of Lien on Real Property should reflect only the reasonable value of the labor, materials, or services furnished for the improvement of the real property. Attorney's fees, lost profits, delay damages, or other consequential damages typically cannot be included as part of the lien amount itself, although they may be recoverable in a separate breach of contract action or if specifically allowed by contract or statute in an enforcement lawsuit.

### Q: Is a pre-lien notice required for a general contractor in North Carolina?

A: No. General contractors in North Carolina have a direct contractual relationship with the property owner and therefore have a "direct lien." They are not required to serve a Notice of Subcontract or a Notice of Claim of Lien Upon Funds as preliminary notices to preserve their lien rights on the real property.
