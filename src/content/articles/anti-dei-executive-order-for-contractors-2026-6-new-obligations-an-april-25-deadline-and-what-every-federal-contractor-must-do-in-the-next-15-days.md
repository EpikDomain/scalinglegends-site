## Breakdown of the Six New Obligations

To ensure your company remains operational and eligible for federal bids, you must understand the specific six obligations codified in the Executive Order. These are not vague guidelines; they are actionable, measurable requirements tied to contract payments.

### 1. Mandatory Demographic Reporting Integration

This is the most visible change. Contractors must report the gender, veteran status, and race/ethnicity breakdown of their workforce on a quarterly basis. Unlike previous voluntary programs, this is mandatory for all subcontractors under the prime contractor. If you are a $10M contractor working under a prime, you are responsible for collecting this data from your $2M tier sub-contractors. Failure to report this data results in a suspension of invoices until compliance is verified. This shifts the burden of data collection down the supply chain, requiring tighter integration with your [prevailing wage calculations](/article/prevailing-wage-calculations/) systems to ensure no worker is missed.

### 2. Safety and Compliance Correlation Logs

The order links safety compliance directly to diversity reporting. You must demonstrate that your safety training hours are distributed equitably. For example, if a specific demographic group is under-represented in your safety leadership roles, you may be flagged for a "diversity compliance violation" even if OSHA citations are zero. This requires you to maintain logs that track not just who was on the job, but who held specific safety roles (e.g., safety officer, first aider).

### 3. Subcontractor Vetting Standards

Before a subcontractor is engaged, they must pass a compliance check using a federal database. This check verifies their own DEI adherence. If your subcontractor is flagged by the federal database, your company's bid score is automatically reduced by 20 points. This creates a ripple effect where you must audit your sub-tier relationships, a process that typically takes 48 hours per sub to verify. In a fast-paced construction environment, this adds friction to the hiring of labor crews.

### 4. Digital Certification Timestamps

All compliance data must be uploaded via a specific digital portal with a timestamp verifying the data was collected within the previous 30 days. Hand-signed forms or PDFs uploaded weeks later are rejected. This demands a shift to real-time data entry. Field foremen must log crew data into a mobile system, which must sync instantly with the central compliance database. This reduces the "lag time" where compliance errors occur before they are discovered.

### 5. Retention of Records for Five Years

The new order extends the retention period for these compliance records from three to five years. This is a storage and legal risk increase. Your data management policies must be updated to ensure digital archives are secure, searchable, and auditable. In a litigation scenario regarding labor disputes, this data will be discoverable, meaning any historical discrepancies can now be used against the company in lawsuits.

### 6. Automated Audit Trails for Wage Disparities

The most technical requirement involves wage data. You must run monthly reports comparing the pay rates of similar roles across demographic lines. If the variance exceeds 5% without a documented, job-specific justification (such as shift differentials or specialized skill requirements), the system flags a potential discrimination claim. This forces an immediate reconciliation of your payroll data against your job descriptions.

## Frequently Asked Questions

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What happens if we miss the April 25 deadline?

Missing the April 25 deadline will result in your company being flagged as non-compliant in the federal portal. You will be ineligible for any new federal contract awards until compliance is verified, which typically takes 3-4 weeks of manual audit. During this time, [cash flow](/article/5-cash-flow-mistakes-that-kill-construction-companies/) from bids will stall.

### Do we need to change our entire payroll system?

Not entirely, but you will need to integrate a layer that can capture the specific diversity and demographic fields required. This can often be done via middleware that feeds data into your existing payroll vendor, rather than replacing the vendor entirely.

### Is this required for private work or only federal?

The Executive Order explicitly applies to federal and federally funded projects. However, many private developers are beginning to require similar compliance data for their own risk mitigation, effectively making it a market-wide standard even for non-federal work.

### How do we report on safety roles specifically?

You must tag specific job roles in your workforce management software with "Safety Responsibilities" (e.g., Safety Officer, First Aider). The system will then aggregate how many individuals in these specific roles belong to which demographic groups.

### Can we use a third-party service to handle the reporting?

Yes, third-party compliance management services exist, but you remain liable for the accuracy of the data. [Smart Business Automator](https://smartbusinessautomator.com) recommends using a tool that you own and control, rather than a hosted service, to ensure long-term data security and portability.

### What is the penalty for a "data mismatch" in wage reporting?

A data mismatch where pay rates differ by more than 5% without documentation will trigger a review. If no justification is found within 10 days, a penalty of $50,000 is applied to the contract. This is often caught automatically during the monthly audit cycle.
